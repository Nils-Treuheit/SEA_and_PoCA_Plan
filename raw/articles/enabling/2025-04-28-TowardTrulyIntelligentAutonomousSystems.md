## Toward Truly Intelligent Autonomous Systems: A Taxonomy of LLM Integration for Everyday Automation
With the rapid development of large language models (LLMs), their integration into autonomous systems has become essential. This integration significantly increases the flexibility and adaptability of the system. In this paper, we propose a categorization of LLM integration into three levels: open-loop, closed-loop, and autonomous systems fully driven by robotic LLMs. They are analyzed through existing literature, real experiments with the humanoid robot TIAGo, and simulations with models such as ChatGPT-4 and Vicuna 13b-v1.5-16k. We demonstrate the potential of LLMs to enhance the flexibility and adaptability of autonomous systems, particularly in dynamic environments where conventional finite state machines may prove inadequate. Closed-loop systems effectively handle unexpected situations with human-like problem solving.
Integrating LLMs with autonomous systems enables new real-world applications by enhancing their ability to adapt, reason, and respond intelligently in dynamic environments.

## Toward Truly Intelligent Autonomous Systems – A Taxonomy of Integrating Large Language Models into Model-Based Systems Engineering

As autonomous systems become increasingly prevalent across various domains, there is a growing need to enhance their intelligence and adaptability beyond traditional rule-based and learning-based approaches. Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language understanding, reasoning, and generation, offering promising opportunities to augment autonomous systems with higher-level cognitive functions.

This paper proposes a taxonomy for integrating large language models (LLMs) into model-based systems engineering (MBSE) workflows to enhance the intelligence and adaptability of autonomous systems. We categorize integration approaches based on their impact on the traditional MBSE V-model and their role in enhancing system capabilities such as reasoning, adaptation, and human-interaction.

### Taxonomy Framework

Our taxonomy consists of three primary dimensions:

1. **Integration Depth**: How deeply LLMs are integrated into the MBSE workflow
   - *Surface Level*: LLMs used for documentation generation and natural language interfaces
   - *Middleware Level*: LLMs used to enhance specific MBSE elements (e.g., requirements, architecture)
   - *Deep Level*: LLMs fundamentally altering the MBSE process itself

2. **Temporal Integration**: When LLMs are applied in the system lifecycle
   - *Design-Time*: LLMs used during system development and modeling phases
   - *Run-Time*: LLMs used during system operation for adaptation and decision-making
   - *Full Lifecycle*: LLMs used throughout both design and runtime phases

3. **Functional Enhancement**: What capabilities LLMs are intended to enhance
   - *Reasoning*: Enhanced logical inference, problem-solving, and decision-making capabilities
   - *Adaptation*: Improved ability to adjust to changing environments and requirements
   - *Human-Interaction*: Better natural language understanding and generation for intuitive interfaces

### Integration Approaches

Based on our taxonomy, we identify seven distinct integration approaches:
- **LLM-Augmented Documentation**: Using LLMs to generate and maintain system documentation
- **LLM-Enhanced Requirements Engineering**: Applying LLMs for requirements elicitation, analysis, and specification
- **LLM-Supported Architectural Design**: Leveraging LLMs for architectural exploration and decision support
- **LLM-Powered Simulation and Testing**: Using LLMs to generate test scenarios and analyze simulation results
- **LLM-Driven Adaptive Control**: Employing LLMs for real-time adaptation and control decisions
- **LLM-Facilitated Human-System Interaction**: Utilizing LLMs for natural language interfaces and interaction
- **LLM-Integrated MBSE Framework**: Fully integrating LLMs into the MBSE process as a core component

Each approach is analyzed in terms of its benefits, challenges, and suitability for different types of autonomous systems and application domains.
