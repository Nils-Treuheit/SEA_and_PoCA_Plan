## 1\. Introduction

Environmental perception is a crucial component of autonomous driving systems, which provides digital driving environment information for the vehicle by perceiving the surrounding environment. The essence of the perception task is the 3D geometric reconstruction of the physical world. Current autonomous driving perception methods perform detection, segmentation, and similar tasks in either the perspective view (PV) or the bird’s-eye view (BEV). BEV has gained significant attention from academia and industry due to its advantages of absolute scale and unobstructed perspective.

The principle of BEV perception involves collecting data about the surrounding environment using onboard sensors, transforming the information into a consistent coordinate system, and ultimately creating an environmental model from a bird’s-eye view perspective. BEV perception, which provides comprehensive environmental information from a bird’s-eye view perspective and helps vehicles more accurately identify the positions of roads, vehicles, obstacles, and pedestrians, enables autonomous vehicles to make more precise and safer decisions. The technology of perception utilizes data from various sensors, including camera, LiDAR and Radar. Each sensor has its own characteristics and advantages, making it suitable for different scenarios.

BEV perception has significant advantages over traditional autonomous driving perception methods based on perspective view or front view. First, BEV avoids common issues in 2D tasks such as occlusion and scale variation. In the perspective view, objects may experience occlusion or scale changes due to perspective (e.g. appearing smaller when farther away), but these problems are significantly mitigated in bird’s-eye view. Secondly, BEV adopts a unified world coordinate system, enabling the integration of data from different sensors, temporal sequences, and spatial information. By converting image and LiDAR point cloud data into the BEV coordinate system for fusion, the vehicle’s perception module combines the semantic information from images with the geometric information from LiDAR. Spatiotemporal fusion further facilitates the perception and prediction of object-motion-states, thereby enhancing the accuracy of autonomous driving perception. Finally, BEV condenses intricate 3D sceneries into a 2D plane while preserving fundamental geometric relationships. It not only significantly decreases the computational complexity of subsequent tasks and promotes model performance, but also improves the efficiency of data annotation and model training. As a result, BEV representation significantly enhances the formulation and deployment of downstream activities, including detection, segmentation, and tracking.

As autonomous driving perception technology advances, various new techniques and methods have emerged. Related methods, concentrate on improving BEV perception to reduce computational costs and improve operational efficiency, while others emphasize integrating features from different sensors and perspectives to obtain more comprehensive data. Consequently, it is essential to summarize and analyze BEV perception methods and compare the various approaches to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the autonomous driving perception field. To this end, we explore BEV perception from the perspective of sensor inputs, focusing on BEV Camera, BEV LiDAR and BEVFusion. The structure of the paper is as follows, with providing an overview of the chapters and their interrelationships. Additionally, we summarize the sensors, datasets, and evaluation metrics used by different methods to facilitate analysis and comparison from multiple perspectives. ### 2.1. Task definition and related work

BEV denotes bird’s eye view, and BEV perception involves converting visual information captured by cameras or LiDAR into BEV features to perform perception tasks such as 3D object detection or map segmentation,. Conventional perspective view based approaches often address perception tasks such as 3D object detection, trajectory prediction, and instance segmentation in isolation, thereby substantially elevating computing expenses. Conversely, BEV perception consolidates tasks into a cohesive algorithmic framework, facilitating effective parallel processing and enhancing computing efficiency. Subsequently, we will present a comprehensive explanation of fundamental principles in BEV technology.

**Detection proposals** 3D object detection is one of the core tasks in BEV perception, which aims to accurately predict the position, size, and class of critical objects (such as vehicles, pedestrians, and obstacles) in three-dimensional space,,. The predicted information can effectively assist in vehicle route planning and enhance obstacle avoidance capabilities. The generation of object proposals play a vital role in improving both the efficiency and accuracy of 3D object detection,. There are two main methods for generating object proposals: grouping methods and window scoring methods.

The core idea of grouping method is to cluster similar elements, such as pixels, shapes, colors, and textures, from image or point cloud data to form target candidate regions. The approach demonstrates relatively high accuracy and strong robustness, but suffers from computational complexity and slow processing speed. Grouping based methods employs hierarchical image segmentation algorithms, which first divide the image into small regions and then progressively merge them into larger areas, finally forming a global segmentation of the image. To increase the number of candidate regions, grouping methods, uses multiple low-level segmentation or over-segmentation followed by a random merging process to diversify the hierarchy. During the random merging of image segmentation results, they typically rely on a set of key cues, including the geometric features of superpixels, color consistency within regions, texture patterns, luminance distribution, and edge contours. Grouping methods can be broadly classified into three categories based on the way candidate regions are generated: superpixels (SP), graph cut (GC), and edge contours (EC). CPMC, adopts a superpixel merging method similar to SelectiveSearch,, progressively combining similar superpixels to generate object proposals. RIGOR, use graph cut (GC) algorithms to partition images into multiple candidate regions. After ranking the features, it utilizes deduplication methods such as the Maximum Margin Relevance (MMR) metric to remove duplicate or similar regions, thereby ensuring the diversity of the candidate regions. Others, extracts edge or contour information from images to segment foreground and background areas, and further divides the image into multiple candidate regions, thereby generating object proposals.

Window scoring method evaluates each candidate window that is likely to contain the target object. In contrast to the grouping method, it exclusively outputs bounding boxes, offering higher computational efficiency. Additionally, window scoring based method provides greater flexibility in handling objects of varying sizes and shapes, but tends to produce candidate boxes with lower precision. Window scoring based method, involves sliding windows at various positions and scales, extracting the features of each window, and evaluating whether these windows are likely to contain the target using a scoring function. Scoring is performed using techniques such as SVM or edge matching, ultimately selecting high-quality candidate boxes.

**Semantic Segmentation** Semantic segmentation plays an indispensable role in BEV perception tasks, which is mainly divided into map segmentation and object segmentation. Map segmentation focuses on extracting static elements such as lane markings and roads to generate BEV maps for path planning,,. Object segmentation targets dynamic elements such as vehicles and pedestrians, providing support for behavior prediction and obstacle avoidance. BEV semantic segmentation not only enhances the depth and breadth of scene understanding but also directly impacts core tasks such as path planning, dynamic decision-making, and multi-modal fusion,. Traditional semantic segmentation is achieved using supervised learning algorithms. Firstly, clustering algorithms are employed to identify the neighborhood of each point for feature extraction, and traditional classifiers are used to perform local classification on each pixel. Then, a conditional random field model is introduced to account for contextual information, enabling global label smoothing and optimization. Finally, the process produces accurate semantic segmentation results. With the rapid development of deep learning, neural networks have demonstrated exceptional efficiency in feature extraction and classification tasks, significantly improving the performance and accuracy of semantic segmentation.

Semantic segmentation methods can be categorized into three types based on the form of input data: point-based methods, grid-based methods, and projection-based methods. Among the most representative point-based semantic segmentation methods are PointNet and PointNet++. PointNet employs shared MLPs to extract features for each point,utilizes pooling operations to aggregate global features, and performs point classification and segmentation by integrating global features with local features. Although PointNet effectively handles point cloud data and provides a global perspective, it may not fully capture local contextual information. To address the limitation, PointNet++ introduces the Set Abstraction module, which performs hierarchical clustering. The approach significantly improves the original PointNet’s ability to capture local contextual information during feature extraction. RandLA-Net significantly reduces the computational complexity of point cloud processing through a random sampling strategy. Compared to PointNet++, RandLA-Net demonstrates superior scalability and real-time processing capabilities when applied to large-scale point cloud data.

Grid-based methods convert point cloud data into a regular grid structure, which is processed using deep neural networks. FCPN employs a uniform sampling strategy for feature extraction and utilizes dense grid representations, which performs point cloud segmentation through a 3D convolutional network combined with a U-Net architecture. LatticeNet addresses the computational efficiency challenges of traditional voxel-based methods in processing large-scale point cloud data by introducing sparse convolution techniques. Additionally, it employs a multi-scale convolutional network to enhance segmentation accuracy.

The projection-based method involves projecting the 3D point cloud onto different 2D planes, performing semantic segmentation on the 2D images, and then projecting the segmentation results back to the 3D space. This process finally yields the point cloud segmentation results. RangeNet++ projects 3D point clouds onto RangeView images and leverages an hourglass network to extract multi-scale features, thereby improving segmentation accuracy. The segmentation results are subsequently back-projected into 3D space and further refined through post-processing. SqueezeSeg projects LiDAR point clouds onto RangeView images and employs a SqueezeNet-based network for semantic segmentation. Post-processing is performed using a conditional random field (CRF), and instance segmentation is achieved through clustering algorithms.

While BEV semantic segmentation plays an important role in scene understanding, it is fundamentally a 2.5D representation that mainly captures the layout of ground objects in the BEV plane. However, the absence of vertical information limits its ability to comprehensively represent 3D spatial semantics. To address this limitation, research has gradually expanded to semantic segmentation in 3D space, namely occupancy-level 3D semantic perception. Unlike BEV semantic segmentation, the OCC 3D semantic segmentation task predicts both the occupancy status and semantic label for each voxel within a 3D voxel grid. This makes it a higher-dimensional and more granular task for spatial scene understanding.

Currently, mainstream occupancy grid ground truth generation methods can be broadly categorized into two types: LiDAR point cloud-based occupancy annotation, and image or depth map-based back-projection supervision. OpenOccupancy adopts a static–dynamic separation strategy, where multi-frame LiDAR point clouds are fused to construct an initial occupancy grid. A pre-trained model is then used to predict a dense occupancy grid, and the two are finally fused to generate a complete 3D occupancy semantic ground truth. Similar to OpenOccupancy, SurroundOcc adopts a pipeline for generating dense occupancy semantic labels. The key difference lies in its use of a nearest-neighbor strategy to assign semantic labels to dense voxels. The approach enables fully automatic generation of 3D occupancy ground truth without the need for human annotation. OCC3D further addresses the handling of occluded regions and uncertain voxels, thereby enhancing the robustness and completeness of the generated labels. NeRF-LiDAR leverages simulation systems to learn more accurate 3D geometry and real-world details, enabling the generation of precise semantic labels. In addition, some methods, attempt to alleviate the difficulty of obtaining occupancy labels by employing a back-projection supervision strategy. Specifically, the predicted occupancy grids are projected into 2D depth maps, which are then supervised using ground-truth depth data. OccNet enhances occupancy and semantic prediction accuracy by leveraging multi-modal fusion that combines the spatial structural awareness inherent in BEV representations with the rich semantic cues provided by image features. In summary, OCC 3D semantic segmentation enables more comprehensive scene understanding and facilitates downstream tasks by building a full 3D semantic representation of complex environments.

**Multi-Sensor Fusion** Multi-sensor fusion is a critical component of BEV perception technology. By integrating the strengths of data from various sensors, BEV perception can construct a more comprehensive representation of the scene,,. In autonomous driving systems, vehicles are typically equipped with multiple sensors, such as cameras, LiDAR and Radar, as well as RGB-D cameras and infrared cameras that have been introduced in certain research studies. Each with distinct characteristics and advantages,while this study primarily focuses on the three most widely used and extensively studied sensors in autonomous driving scenarios: cameras, LiDAR and Radar. Cameras provide high-resolution RGB images containing rich color and texture information, which are beneficial for object recognition and classification. However, cameras cannot directly acquire depth information, making precise object localization challenging. LiDAR sensors offer significant advantages in providing high-precision depth information and perceiving environmental structures. However, adverse weather conditions can affect their performance. Additionally, LiDAR point cloud data is relatively sparse and lacks rich color and texture information. Radar excels in detecting objects at long ranges and demonstrates strong anti-interference capabilities, allowing it to operate effectively under complex weather conditions. However, Radar has relatively low perception accuracy, and its data is sparse, lacking the ability to provide detailed information on object shapes and textures. Fusing data from different modalities enables the complementary strengths of various sensors to be leveraged, thereby capturing and utilizing environmental information more comprehensively,,.

In addition, the fusion of LiDAR, Radar, RGB cameras, as well as RGB-D cameras and infrared cameras represents a promising direction for further exploration. Nevertheless, existing studies in this area remain relatively limited. RGB-D cameras are capable of generating dense depth maps, excelling in near-field scenarios, and providing significant value for object detection, localization, and navigation. However, the sensing range is limited to only a few meters and they may be affected by low-texture areas and challenging lighting conditions. In contrast, infrared cameras can detect temperature differences between objects and their surroundings under low-light or low-visibility conditions (e.g., at night or in fog), and convert these differences into thermal images. However, infrared cameras typically have lower resolution than RGB cameras and are more expensive. As a result, they have not yet been widely adopted and remain primarily in the experimental research phase.

Currently, multi-sensor fusion strategies can be broadly categorized into two main types: perception-level fusion and decision-level fusion. Perception-level fusion involves combining raw data or intermediate features from multiple sensors at the perception stage of the model, aiming to achieve more accurate and robust perception outcomes. Perception-level fusion can be classified into early fusion and intermediate fusion, depending on the stage at which data from different sensors are integrated. The principles of these fusion stages are illustrated in.

In early fusion, the raw data collected from different sensors are first synchronized and then directly fused before being fed into the perception model. VPFNet, enhances the expressive power of LiDAR point clouds by incorporating semantic information from images during the input stage. Data-level fusion retains the most information, but the heterogeneity between camera and LiDAR data leads to challenges such as coordinate transformation difficulties and high computational overhead, making it complex to implement. Intermediate fusion, also referred to as feature-level fusion, integrates features extracted independently from LiDAR and images during the feature extraction stage. Wang proposed a method that extracts features from both LiDAR point clouds and images, followed by deep-level fusion to effectively integrate complementary information from the two modalities, leading to a significant improvement in detection performance. Feature-level fusion is widely adopted in BEV perception due to its advantages such as minimal data loss and low computational overhead.

Late fusion, also known as decision-level fusion, refers to the strategy where each sensor independently performs object detection, and the results are subsequently fused at the decision level. MV3D projects LiDAR point clouds onto the BEV map and utilizes a Region Proposal Network (RPN) to generate candidate object regions. SE-SSD is proposed based on MV3D, generating object proposals from BEV maps and feature maps extracted from RGB images. TransFusion generates a set of image proposals and projects their features into the 3D frustum space for object detection. The type of proposal-level fusion is typically applied in multi-stage detection pipelines and is inherently object-centric, which limits its extensibility to other tasks such as BEV map segmentation,.

### 2.3. Evaluation metrics

**Mean Average Precision (mAP)** is a key metric for evaluating object detection tasks in BEV perception. In traditional object detection, Average Precision (AP) is determined by calculating the Intersection over Union (IoU) between predicted boxes and ground truth boxes. However, in the BEV perspective, the complexity of positional and geometric relationships in three-dimensional space makes IoU computation particularly challenging. To address this challenge, researchers have adopted the distance between the center of the detection box and the center of the ground-truth box as a substitute for IoU, thereby reducing the error caused by bounding box offset. Typically, a detection distance threshold is set, and the detection is considered correct when the detected distance is less than the given threshold. By setting a series of confidence thresholds, corresponding precision and recall values can be calculated. These values are used to plot the Precision–Recall (PR) curve, and the AP is computed by integrating the area under the PR curve. (1) $A P = \int_{0}^{1} \text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right) d r$

$\text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right)$ denotes the precision at a given recall rate $r$. Finally, the mean Average Precision (mAP) is obtained by averaging the AP values calculated across all thresholds. In addition, Waymo Open proposed the AP Hungarian algorithm and incorporated directional weighting into the AP metric, introducing the APH metric to enhance evaluation precision. KITTI uses the PASCAL standard to evaluate object detection performance in the BEV perspective.

**NuScenes Detection Score (NDS)** is a comprehensive evaluation metric proposed in the nuScenes dataset. This evaluation mechanism integrates mAP with multiple attribute error metrics — mean Translation Error (mATE), mean Scale Error (mASE), mean Orientation Error (mAOE), mean Velocity Error (mAVE), and mean Attribute Error (mAAE) — to prevent models from over-optimizing a single metric while neglecting other critical factors. As shown in Equ,, the comprehensive metric enables simultaneous evaluation of detection accuracy, geometric attributes, and dynamic properties, making it more aligned with the requirements of autonomous driving (2) $\text{TP}_{\text{score}} = max \left(\right. 1 - \text{TP}_{\text{error}} , 0 . 0 \left.\right)$ (3) $\text{NDS} = \frac{5 \cdot \text{mAP} + \sum_{i = 1}^{5} \text{TP}_{\text{score}}^{i}}{10}$

**Mean Intersection over Union (mIOU)** is a standard metric for evaluating semantic segmentation, frequently applied to assess the performance of 3D occupancy prediction models. Similar to 2D pixel-level semantic segmentation, mIoU measures the degree of overlap between the predicted results and the ground truth labels. mIoU effectively reflects the model’s accuracy in recognizing various objects within a scene. The formula for mIoU is as follows: (4) $\text{mIoU} = \frac{1}{C} \times \sum_{i = 1}^{c} \frac{T P_{i}}{T P_{i} + F P_{i} + F N_{i}}$

$C$ denotes the number of semantic classes, and $T P_{i}$, $F P_{i}$, and $F N_{i}$ represent the true positives, false positives, and false negatives for class $i$, respectively. When semantic labels are not considered, $\text{IoU}$ can also serve as an evaluation metric to measure the accuracy of occupancy prediction in space. (5) $\text{IoU} = \frac{T P}{T P + F P + F N}$

**LET-3D-APL** is a metric specifically designed for evaluating 3D object detection, which emphasizes tolerance and weighting of longitudinal errors. This metric allows predicted boxes to exhibit longitudinal localization errors within a given tolerance range and effectively mitigates such errors by introducing longitudinal affinity to weight the precision. The calculation formula is as follows: (6) $\text{LET}-\text{3D}-\text{APL} = \int_{0}^{1} \text{Prec}_{L} \left(\right. r \left.\right) d r = \int_{0}^{1} \overset{̄}{a}_{l} \cdot \text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right) d r$

$\text{Prec}_{L} \left(\right. r \left.\right)$ is the longitudinal affinity-weighted precision, $\text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right)$ is the precision at recall rate $r$, and $\overset{̄}{a}_{l}$ is the average longitudinal affinity.

### 3.1. Camera-centric BEV perception

Camera-only perception has garnered significant attention from both academia and industry. Compared to perception solutions that rely on high-precision LiDAR, camera-only BEV perception systems offer a more cost-effective alternative while maintaining competitive performance. However, the approach presents an inherent challenge:the imaging process of cameras results in the loss of partial 3D information, which prevents the acquisition of accurate depth information and thus hinders precise target localization. As shown in, camera-only 3D perception framework mainly consists of three components:the image-view encoder is primarily used to process the input RGB images and extract features;the view transformation module converts image features into BEV features;the BEV decoder further decodes the transformed features. Finally, the generated BEV features are applied to specific tasks. The view transformation module is crucial in pure vision-based BEV perception, as it lifts 2D features into 3D space generating BEV features for downstream tasks such as object detection and semantic segmentation,.

View transformation methodologies can be categorized into three principal approaches. The first method is the ’2D–3D methods’, which predicts the depth distribution of pixels from 2D image features and then ‘lifts’ these 2D features into 3D space based on the estimated depth information. The second method is the ’3D-2D methods’, which populates the corresponding 3D voxel grid with 2D image features based on constraints from the camera’s intrinsic and extrinsic parameters, along with depth estimation. The third category employs an ’end-to-end network architecture,’ which discards geometric prior constraints and directly leverages BEV-annotated data to train deep neural networks, thereby establishing an implicit mapping between image pixels and BEV grids.

By comparing different methods, we observe that SimpleBEV significantly outperforms traditional depth estimation (DE) methods, indicating that its simplified depth estimation design provides greater advantages in accuracy and robustness. Compared to dense 3D grid representations, sparse voxel representations achieve higher accuracy with lower computational resource consumption. In pure network approaches, optimization of network architectures is critical for performance improvement. #### 3.1.1. 2D-to-3D view transformation

The 2D–3D method involves predicting the depth position of each pixel based on 2D image features. By leveraging depth information, the related method enables the understanding of an object’s position in 3D space, thereby facilitating the transformation from 2D images to 3D space. LSS is a typical 2D–3D transformation method. The approach ‘lifts’ 2D image features into the 3D space based on the predicted depth information, then ‘projects’ the generated 3D point cloud features onto the rasterized BEV grid to achieve the perspective transformation. The specific conversion process is as follows: First, the discrete depth probability distribution $\alpha_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right)$ is predicted at each pixel (u, v). Then, $\alpha_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right)$ is multiplied with the 2D image features C(u, v) to obtain the voxel features at each depth layer. (7) $\mathbf{c}_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right) = \alpha_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right) \cdot \mathbf{C} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right)$ Subsequently, these features are geometrically transformed $M_{\text{trans}}$ into the 3D voxel coordinate system based on the camera intrinsics $\mathbf{K}$ and extrinsics $\left[\right. \mathbf{R} \left|\right. \mathbf{T} \left]\right.$, resulting in 3D spatial features $\mathcal{F}_{3 D} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right)$. (8) $\mathcal{F}_{\text{3D}} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right) = M_{\text{trans}} \left(\right. \mathcal{F}_{\text{2D}} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right) , \mathbf{K} , \left[\right. \mathbf{R} \mid \mathbf{T} \left]\right. \left.\right)$ Finally, the generated 3D spatial features $\mathcal{F}_{3 D} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right)$ are projected along the height axis to obtain the BEV representation $F_{\text{BEV}} \left(\right. x , y \left.\right)$. (9) $F_{\text{BEV}} \left(\right. x , y \left.\right) = \underset{z}{\sum} F_{3 D} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right)$

However, this process inevitably introduces depth estimation errors, which mainly stem from inaccurate predictions of depth probability distributions, quantization errors caused by depth discretization, and depth discontinuities. Specifically, inaccurate depth distribution prediction leads to projection deviations in the BEV feature space; discretizing depth into a fixed number of bins introduces quantization errors; and the instability between discrete depth bins and softmax outputs results in spatial discontinuities of the 3D projected points, giving rise to the phenomenon of “depth jumping”. Such errors directly affect the accuracy of BEV representations, thereby degrading the performance and robustness of downstream tasks such as detection and segmentation. To address this issue, subsequent studies based on the LSS framework have proposed various methods to mitigate these errors.

Based on the LSS paradigm, BEVDet employs a similar architecture that predicts depth through a classification-based approach and significantly improves depth prediction accuracy by supervising depth estimation with LiDAR reference data. Furthermore, the paper proposes a scale non-maximum suppression(NMS) method that effectively addresses redundancy issues in small object detection, offering novel insights for 3D object detection. Although the depth estimation of LSS paradigm struggles to meet high-precision 3D detection requirements in certain scenarios, the method can obtain explicit BEV features for downstream tasks. BEVDepth significantly enhances depth estimation accuracy through explicit depth supervision and further optimizes predictions by incorporating a depth refinement module.

MambaOcc builds upon the LSS paradigm for view transformation, which extracts 2D image features through an image backbone network and generates discrete depth values for each pixel. The key distinction between LSS and MambaOcc lies in the latter’s use of a voxel pooling method to directly aggregate depth prediction results, thus simplifying the depth feature fusion strategy. To simplify model architectures and improve computational efficiency, some approaches, employ bilinear sampling to lift image features into 3D voxel space, where the features implicitly encode depth-related information. While processing multi-view images, LSS may not fully exploit the correlations between adjacent surround-view images, resulting in fragmented extraction of image features. To address these limitations, GraphBEV introduces a LocalAlign module that enhances depth feature perception through graph matching with neighboring context, improving the accuracy of feature transformation.

#### 3.1.2. 3D-to-2D view transformation

In addition to the explicit depth distribution estimation strategies used in LSS and BEVDet, another approach refers to ’3D-2D methods’, which projects 2D image features into corresponding voxels through geometric mapping. Based on depth estimation and camera intrinsics parameters, each voxel aggregates image features from its projected regions on the image plane. OFT represents a representative 3D-2D method, which constructs a uniformly distributed 3D voxel grid, accumulating image features from the projected regions. The orthographic BEV feature map is then generated by flattening voxel features in the height direction. When compressing the voxel grid in the height dimension, a weighted averaging approach is typically adopted to aggregate information from multiple height layers into a single BEV feature to reduce computational load. This method not only decreases the computational cost of depth estimation but also fully utilizes depth information in the scene.

Recently, the combination of 3D-2D projection and neural network has become increasingly popular. Because neural network can efficiently extract and process image features, especially when dealing with complex scenes and dynamic environments. For example, the cross-attention mechanism in Transformer can adaptively focus on key regions in 3D space and associate these key points with 2D image features across modalities, which significantly enhances the robustness of features in complex dynamic scenes. Some approaches,, utilize the intrinsic and extrinsic camera matrices to project a set of 3D query points onto the planes of multi-view images and aggregate image features to achieve 3D object detection. Additionally, researchers have designed a projected cross-attention mechanism to address the issues of insufficient utilization of geometric cues and loss of cross-view object information in existing methods. BEVFormer employs the cross-attention mechanism to align 3D query points with 2D image features, achieving efficient 3D-2D view transformation. Based on BEVFormer, BEVFormerV2 introduces a perspective supervision mechanism. By adding auxiliary detection heads to the image backbone network to supervise multi-view images, it significantly enhances the geometric consistency of BEV features. BEVMamba introduces Mamba for temporal modeling, thereby improving upon the BEVFormer paradigm that relies on attention mechanisms for temporal modeling. This improvement strategy effectively addresses the issue of low efficiency when attention mechanisms handle long sequences. Other related methods,, employ convolutional networks to extract features from multi-view images and utilize projective network modules to implicitly learn 3D-to −2D geometric correspondences for generating robust BEV representations

However, BEVFormer and some networks on depth information prediction consume substantial computational resources. To enhance computational efficiency and network performance, Fast-BEV introduces a lightweight and deployment-friendly view transformation. The related method obtains the BEV representation by projecting multi-view 2D image features along camera rays onto a 3D grid, which significantly enhances computational efficiency. Furthermore, MetaBEV also introduces a cross-modal attention mechanism to dynamically adjust the feature fusion strategy, addressing the issue of sensor temporal validity and ensuring high-quality BEV feature generation even in cases of sensor damage or absence.

#### 3.1.3. Pure network-based methods for BEV generation

Both ’2D-to −3D methods’ and ’3D-to −2D methods’ rely on the inherited inductive bias in geometric projection, which makes the geometric models dependent on complex parameter tuning and hinders large-scale deployment. In comparison, pure network-based approaches tend to utilize multi-layer perceptron mechanism,, or Transformer,, architecture to implicitly represent the mapping from camera views to the bird’s-eye-view. These architectures enable end-to-end training and eliminate the dependency on explicit depth estimation. MLP serves as a mapping function, accepting data from different sensors as input and producing corresponding BEV outputs, thereby facilitating efficient transformations from PV to BEV. VPN utilizes MLP for processing multi-camera views to generate bird’s-eye-view. And the related method supports the accumulation of information over multiple time steps, enhancing the stability and robustness of the map. PON employs the concept of column-wise view transformation, firstly extracting multi-scale image features from a feature pyramid. Subsequently, PON folds features along the vertical direction through MLP and expands them along the depth axis, ultimately accomplishing the view transformation as shown in. On the basis of PON, BEV-LANEDET has designed a lightweight view transformation module based on MLP, which converts multi-scale front-view feature maps into BEV representations, achieving more accurate and efficient 3D lane detection. VectorMapNet introduces a two-layer MLP that learns the projection relationship between input embeddings and outputs, enabling the prediction of keypoint locations and category labels. The method lays a solid foundation for the generation of vectorized maps.

While MLP has achieved remarkable results in the view transformation from PV to BEV, it faces challenges in effectively handling the global positional relationships among multiple views. In contrast, the attention mechanism in the Transformer architecture enables cross-view learning, which facilitates a better understanding of global positional information. Furthermore, the robust data dependency and scalability of the Transformer allows them to learn more flexible feature representations, enabling the handling of more complex scenes and adaptation to varying task requirements. CVT embeds positional information through the internal and external calibration of cameras and introduces a cross-view attention mechanism to achieve the projection from camera view to BEV. DETR3D employs a Transformer architecture to enhance BEV features for better predictions. Bev-locator projects multi-view image features into the BEV space. Additionally, the method introduces a cross-modal Transformer module, which infers the ego pose by computing self-attention and cross-attention. DiffBEV proposes an end-to-end framework that utilizes a cross-attention module to merge BEV features. However, the Transformer-based approaches adopt unidirectional and posterior interaction mechanisms, which pose challenges for the transformation from perspective view to bird’s eye view. To address this issue, BAEFormer proposes a bi-directional and early interaction transformers framework, which further enhances the algorithm’s performance by downsampling multiview image features. The implicit methods no longer rely on explicit geometric transformations for the projection from PV to BEV, but instead focus more on training neural networks with large amounts of data to achieve efficient view transformation.

#### 3.2.1. Voxel-based methods

Voxel-based methods discretize 3D point cloud data into voxels, thus providing an efficient and structured representation for 3D scene understanding.VoxelNet discretizes the point cloud data using voxel methods, and then compresses the extracted voxel features into the BEV space to perform detection and segmentation tasks. VoxelNeXt adopts the core concept of VoxelNet, converting point cloud features into BEV representations after feature extraction and ultimately performing 3D detection tasks. As illustrated in, the primary distinction between VoxelNeXt and VoxelNet lies in their feature transformation strategies: VoxelNet utilizes max-pooling operations to compress 3D voxel features into BEV space, necessitating intermediate dense tensor conversions and NMS post-processing. In contrast, VoxelNeXt adopts a fully sparse convolutional approach that directly bridges 3D sparse features to BEV representations through parameterized geometric projection, eliminating the need for dense tensor transformations and NMS stages. The architectural innovation significantly reduces computational complexity while maintaining competitive accuracy.

Existing methods,, predominantly focus on single-frame point cloud data, neglecting the spatiotemporal information inherent in sequential LiDAR point clouds. To fully exploit the valuable temporal cues in time-series data, InsMos takes a sequence of LiDAR point clouds as input and incorporates temporal information through 4D voxel quantization. The framework subsequently leverages sparse convolution to extract motion-aware features from these 4D voxels for instance detection. Park proposes a 3D object detection framework, which enriches the representation of voxel features by designing a temporal voxel encoder to capture the temporal relationships between point sets within voxels. The method then analyzes discrepancies between consecutive BEV feature maps to extract motion information, which is integrated into the current BEV features to achieve more accurate 3D object detection. KAN-RCBEVDepth aligns point cloud features from multiple temporal frames to the current coordinate system, enhancing the accuracy of feature representation. Furthermore, the paper proposes an optimized voxel pooling method that efficiently aggregates multi-modal features into the BEV and leverages CUDA thread-based parallel processing of voxels, significantly improving computational efficiency.

In 3D object detection tasks, LiDAR point clouds exhibit an uneven distribution in 3D space, resulting in significant disparities in their contributions to object detection across different spatial regions. To address the non-uniformity of point cloud distributions, Focal Sparse Conv first predicts critical regions of importance in 3D space and then extracts features using sparse convolution, substantially enhancing the capability of convolutional networks. Building upon Focal Sparse Conv, MDRNet proposes a point cloud detection network based on a multi-stage feature dimensionality reduction strategy. During feature transformation, the network dynamically focuses on regions with high data contribution, thereby mitigating information loss and reducing computational overhead. SlENet proposes a spatial information enhancement network that demonstrates robustness to density-imbalanced point clouds, achieving precise 3D object detection.

#### 3.2.2. Pillar-based methods

The core of the Pillar-based method lies in organizing sparse LiDAR point clouds into regular pillar structures, thereby streamlining the feature extraction pipeline. Specifically, the point cloud data is first discretized into a 2D grid, and then the extracted pillar features are mapped into a pseudo-image to generate BEV features. Compared to voxel-based methods, pillar-based methods significantly reduce computational resource consumption, achieving higher computational efficiency and real-time performance. PointPillars pioneered a pillar-based feature extraction paradigm, which partitions point clouds into vertical pillar structures. This approach learns latent representations through pseudo-image encoding and executes downstream perception tasks. However, PointPillars and other pillar-based methods,, suffer from limited pillar feature encoding capabilities, struggling to detect distant or small-sized objects. To enhance small object detection performance, pillarNet designs a more powerful encoding network for pillar feature learning and incorporates multi-scale feature fusion, thus better capturing local geometric details. MVF introduces an end-to-end multi-view fusion algorithm that integrates bird’s-eye view and perspective-view features to learn more effective pillar representations, enhancing 3D object detection performance. Fastpillars introduces a lightweight max-and-attention pillar encoding module that enhances feature representation for small 3D objects by aggregating critical local geometric patterns. Additionally, the paper designs a deployment-friendly pillar-based 3D detector capable of achieving high-performance and low-latency object detection without relying on sparse convolutions.

To further advance real-time performance, FlatFormer designs a flattened window attention mechanism, effectively addressing the computational bottlenecks of point cloud Transformers when processing 3D point clouds and narrowing the latency gap. PointBeV adopts an efficient two-stage sparse computation strategy, which significantly reduces computational redundancy while maintaining accuracy, and enables fast adaptation to different platforms through flexible memory control. MotionBEV converts 3D LiDAR scans into 2D polar BEV representations, significantly boosting computational efficiency. The method also adaptively fuse spatiotemporal information from appearance and motion features, thereby enhancing the accuracy of temporal prediction. CenterPoint employs a network architecture similar to PointPillars to construct a representation of the input point cloud, then flattens these features into the BEV feature map for object detection. By representing 3D objects as points, it significantly simplifies target recognition and further improves real-time performance.

Additionally, Pillar-od proposes a pillar-based method to address anchor-induced imbalance issues. By integrating cylindrical projection into multi-view feature learning and predicting bounding box parameters per pillar rather than per point, this approach optimizes prediction accuracy and enhances detection performance. Both voxel-based and pillar-based methods partition 3D space into discrete regions, each containing subsets of point cloud data. Voxel-based approaches discretize irregular point clouds into regular voxel grids, enabling direct application of 3D convolutional neural networks for feature extraction. However, this process often introduces numerous empty voxels, resulting in inefficient computational resource utilization. In contrast, pillar-based methods offer greater flexibility by organizing point clouds into vertical pillar structures. Each pillar encapsulates point cloud information within a defined spatial range. And the pillar dimensions can be adaptively adjusted based on task requirements, thereby significantly improving computational efficiency.

#### 3.3.1. Camera–LiDAR fusion

Multi-sensor fusion enables complementary advantages across different sensing modalities, allowing systems to holistically capture environmental information. Camera data provides dense color and texture information but lacks accurate depth perception, whereas LiDAR delivers precise depth measurements information at the cost of sparse data density and limited perception range. Multi-modal fusion can preserve the semantic information of images and the geometric information of LiDAR, obtaining more comprehensive environmental information. BEVFusion represents a representative Camera–LiDAR fusion approach, which employs a separate dual-branch framework to generate BEV representations from LiDAR and camera data respectively as shown in. The approach fuses these multi-modal feature maps through cross-view interaction, and performs downstream perception tasks including object detection and map segmentation. Based on BEVFusion, BEVFusion4D designs a LiDAR-guided view transformation module, which leverages the spatial cues from LiDAR to more accurately extract semantic information from images, thereby obtaining more effective BEV representations. Additionally, BEVFusion4D utilizes temporal information to aggregate BEV features from multiple historical frames, achieving more accurate perception.

Most related methods still face the challenge of inaccurate depth estimation in areas with significant depth changes. To alleviate the depth jump problem, BEVDepth aligns the depth estimates with the projected depth of the point cloud through minimum pooling and one-hot operations. EA-LSS proposes a edge-aware LSS framework, which enhances the fine-grained supervision of depth by adjusting the size of the predicted depth map and the ground-truth depth map, enabling the model to better adapt to rapid depth changes between objects. Current 3D perception methods mainly use modality-specific encoders to extract features from different sensors and then transform them into a unified BEV space. However, this process leads to additional computational overhead. To reduce the consumption of computational resources, FUTR3D designs a modality-agnostic feature sampler that can sample and aggregate features from different sensors, providing a low-cost and high-performance perception system for autonomous driving. UniTR introduces a modality-agnostic transformer encoder, which simplifies the network architecture by processing different modality feature representations in parallel and enabling automatic cross-modal interaction. Although multi-sensor fusion is increasingly gaining attention in this field, it is still affected by poor image conditions. To address this issue, TransFusion proposes a robust Camera–LiDAR fusion method that uses the attention mechanism of the Transformer to adaptively extract image information, generating a stable and effective fusion strategy. CMT proposes a hybrid network based on the Transformer to capture long-range dependencies in images, improving the overall accuracy of the model.

Although current multi-modal BEV perception methods haveachieved remarkable results on standard datasets such as nuScenes and KITTI, they still face numerous challenges in real-world deployment. In terms of calibration, CalibRBEV proposed an automatic multi-camera calibration method based on inverse BEV representations. In terms of annotation, high-quality data labeling is fundamental for training and evaluating BEV perception models. However, traditional 3D annotation methods struggle to capture temporal continuity and object motion trajectories in dynamic scenes. To address this, 4D annotation techniques have emerged, introducing the time dimension on top of 3D spatial labeling, enabling a more comprehensive description of object trajectories and behavioral changes. Regarding model fine-tuning, BEV perception systems often need to adapt to different scenarios and tasks. However, directly training models in new environments can face challenges such as insufficient data and high annotation costs. To address this, researchers have proposed a BEV perception network fine-tuning method based on visual 2D semantic perception. The approach leverages mature 2D perception techniques to reduce reliance on costly BEV annotation data, significantly improving the model’s generalization ability in new scenarios. Additionally, BEVGPT enhances the adaptability of BEV models by performing online fine-tuning of pre-trained models within autonomous driving simulation environments, enabling dynamic prediction of future trajectories.

#### 3.3.2. Temporal information fusion

Temporal information plays a significant role in predicting the motion state of objects and detecting occluded objects. By integrating data from multiple historical frames, temporal fusion compensates for the limitations of single-frame perception and significantly improves the accuracy of perception algorithms,. As illustrated in, BEVFormer employs predefined grid-shaped BEV queries to interact with spatiotemporal information, generating a unified BEV representation. Additionally, the approach introduces a temporal self-attention module to fuse historical BEV information, further enhancing the BEV features. TempBEV, proposes an effective mechanism for fusing data from different views. The mechanism aggregates temporal information from both the image latent space and the BEV latent space and leverages optical flow estimation for temporal stereo encoding, achieving higher detection accuracy and robustness. BEVFormer and some LSS-based methods primarily rely on transforming the image view into a Cartesian BEV representation. These methods overlook the non-uniform distribution of image information and find it hard to leverage view symmetry.

To address these limitations, PolarDETR proposes replacing Cartesian BEV representations with polar BEV representations. Under this polar coordinate framework, PolarDETR projects the sampling anchor points of the current frame to previous frames to retrieve historical image features. Subsequently, the method aggregates features from the current frame and past frames to update the query embeddings. The temporal fusion mechanism enables more effective capture of dynamic object cues for enhanced motion-aware perception. Based on the PolarDETR paradigm, PolarBEVDet leverages ego-motion to align historical features with the current timestamp for temporal fusion, achieving superior performance in multi-view 3D object detection tasks.

MGTANet designs a 3D object detection architecture, which introduces short-term motion-aware voxel feature encoding to obtain BEV feature maps. Additionally, the method proposes a long-term feature aggregation method to guide BEV feature enhancement, constructing complementary short-term and long-term temporal fusion. BEVDepth, employs a multi-frame fusion mechanism that aligns the coordinates of the view frustum features from different frames with the current ego-coordinate system, eliminating the influence of ego-motion. Such alignment enables the model to handle dynamic scenes more accurately.

### 3.4. BEV enhancement techniques

#### 3.4.1. Prior information filtering

In the field of autonomous driving perception, multi-sensor fusion is crucial for achieving accurate environmental perception. But data from different sensors vary in terms of accuracy, timestamps, perspectives, and may contain noise in the raw data. These factors may interfere with subsequent feature extraction and model prediction. To ensure data consistency and accuracy, preprocessing through pre-information filtering is necessary to reduce the interference of invalid data on BEV feature projection. However, existing methods are susceptible to inaccuracies in sensor calibration, leading to misalignment between LiDAR and camera BEV features. Some methods employ a cross-attention mechanism to allow features from different modalities to guide each other for alignment, mitigating the feature misalignment issue caused by projection matrix errors between LiDAR and cameras. ContrastAlign proposes utilizing contrastive learning to enhance the alignment of heterogeneous modalities, improving the robustness of the fusion process. Building upon ContrastAlign, GraphBEV introduces the LocalAlign module and the GlobalAlign module. The LocalAlign module primarily relies on graph matching and adopts locally aware depth features to enrich contextual depth feature learning. The GlobalAlign module employs learnable offsets to achieve global multi-modal feature alignment. Other methods add a deformable alignment layer in the multi-modal fusion network, using the flexibility of deformable convolution to dynamically compensate for calibration offsets.

Interferences in complex road environments may lead to the loss or incompleteness of key frames, impacting the overall perception capability. Against this background, Co-Fix3D employs an attention mechanism to filter out important features, further enhancing the overall perception capability. To maintain efficient computation while aggregating contextual information, SparseDet adopts a sparse sampling strategy, which selectively aggregates voxel features within regions of interest. The related approach not only addresses the issue of insufficient information representation in traditional sparse detection methods but also significantly enhances inference speed. RoadBEV aligns and calibrates left and right images to ensure consistency between views, reducing noise caused by sensor calibration errors and perspective differences. On the other hand, RopeBEV introduces the region of interest mask module, which filters out invalid perception areas by defining regions of interest. The filtering process ensures that the input feature set is more focused on key features relevant to the object detection task, thus enhancing the efficiency and accuracy of subsequent processing. This process enhances the performance of 3D object detection.

#### 3.4.2. Enhancement of intermediate layer features

The enhancement of intermediate layer features aims to optimize feature representation to address the challenges of viewpoint changes and complex scenes, thereby improving the model’s perception performance in the BEV space. Specific enhancement techniques include: multi-modal feature fusion to enhance the complementarity of modal information, the introduction of attention mechanisms to focus on key areas and suppress redundant information, and feature alignment optimization to address geometric distortions caused by perspective differences. Furthermore, diffusion models have emerged as a powerful feature enhancement approach due to their strengths in feature reconstruction and denoising. BEVWorld employs a noise-based diffusion process within the BEV latent space to iteratively optimize feature distributions, gradually improving the sharpness of feature representations and the accuracy of downstream predictions. BEVDiffuser utilizes real-world object layouts as guidance to effectively denoise the BEV feature maps. To further enhance semantic granularity, DiffBEV introduces a conditional diffusion model that guides coarse BEV features toward a more fine-grained semantic space. In addition, DifFUSER models multi-modal fusion as a diffusion process, enabling automatic feature completion under sensor failure or degradation, which significantly enhances the robustness and adaptability of the fusion results.

Although multi-modal fusion is widely favored in the field of autonomous driving, existing fusion methods are highly susceptible to poor image conditions. These impacts can lead insufficient robustness of multi-modal fusion when facing complex environmental conditions. TransFusion proposes a robust LiDAR–camera fusion strategy that tackles poor image quality through a soft association mechanism. Additionally, the method utilizes an attention mechanism to adaptively extract target information from images, achieving robust and effective fusion results. To more effectively handle complex spatio-temporal relationships, QE-BEV introduces the Top-K Attention mechanism. The mechanism adaptively focuses on clusters of highly relevant features in the context, significantly enhancing the expressive power of BEV features. Additionally, the paper proposes a diversity loss function that pays attention to both important and less prominent features, further improving the model’s robustness in complex environments. BEV-IO enhances the BEV representation through instance occupancy information. Specifically, the method performs self-attention operations based on the occupancy distribution of each ray in the frustum, thereby effectively aggregating the geometric cues of instances into the image features. This significantly improves the expressiveness and semantic richness of the BEV features.

#### 3.4.3. Output feature optimization

The optimization of output features aims to post-process the generated BEV features, making them more suitable for downstream tasks. However, key challenges such as the difficulty in perceiving small targets and the decline in cross-domain generalization performance still exist in real-world scenarios. Factors such as perceptual noise, information sparsity, and object occlusion often affect the accuracy of small object detection, posing potential threats to the safety of autonomous driving. To address these issues, BEV-TinySpotter introduces multi-scale spatial information, significantly enhancing the feature recognition capability for small objects. Additionally, this method incorporates temporal sequence information to capture the distribution changes and movement trends of small objects across consecutive frames, improving the understanding of their dynamic characteristics. On the other hand, different domains may have varying data distributions or environmental conditions. To mitigate the impact of performance degradation across domains, BEV-Seg generates cross-domain intermediate representations by abstracting high-level geometric features, effectively addressing the performance degradation issues caused by domain differences in traditional methods. The related approach can adapt to different domains and maintain good performance in new domains while reducing dependence on large-scale labeled data, thereby significantly enhancing the reliability and accuracy of the output features.

To further improve depth perception, EA-BEV optimizes depth estimation through its edge-aware deep fusion module. This module leverages edge information to enhance the depth reasoning of object contours, improving the local details of depth estimation. Such enhancement optimizes the BEV feature representation and bolsters the reliability of output features in terms of spatial consistency and geometric accuracy. BEVRefiner proposes a deep refinement method that accurately predicts the depth information of each pixel by integrating 3D context information and prior knowledge of depth distribution, enhancing the quality of the generated BEV features. Most existing methods aggregate multi-view camera features into the BEV grid, but these approaches may ignore the feature differences of targets at different heights, leading to information loss or confusion. To alleviate the issue of information loss, BEV-SAN subdivides the BEV space into multiple height slices, generating global and local BEV slices separately to preserve the feature differences of targets at different heights. Additionally, BEV-SAN utilizes an attention mechanism to dynamically assign weights, highlighting key features, thereby enhancing the accuracy and robustness of the feature representation.

## 4\. Future outlook and challenges

**Multi-modal Fusion** With the development of sensor technologies, future BEV perception techniques will increasingly rely on multi-sensor data fusion, particularly the collaborative work of cameras, LiDAR, and Radar. This multi-sensor fusion can compensate for the limitations of individual sensors in complex environments, enhancing the accuracy, robustness, and reliability of perception. Specifically, LiDAR provides high-precision depth information, maintaining good performance even in low light conditions,. Radar has an advantage in complex weather conditions such as rain, fog, and snow due to its strong penetration capabilities through water vapor and particles in the environment. Cameras, on the other hand, offer high-resolution visual information, making them especially suitable for object recognition and detail capture.

**Generative and 4D BEV Modeling** The BEV perception system will no longer be limited to a single object detection task but is expected to expand to a wider range of perception tasks, such as semantic segmentation, path planning, trajectory prediction, and multi-object tracking. These perception tasks further raise the requirements for the modeling capabilities and generalization performance of BEV systems. To address these challenges, BEV research is evolving toward deeper and more diverse directions. Firstly, diffusion models have been introduced into BEV perception due to their powerful denoising capabilities, enabling the generation of higher-quality BEV representations. Secondly, 4D BEV modeling has also gained increasing attention, as it incorporates the temporal dimension into the traditional 3D spatial framework, empowering autonomous driving systems with a better understanding of dynamic scenes.

**LLM-Augmented BEV Perception** Meanwhile, the integration of large language models (LLMs) with BEV perception has emerged as a new research hotspot. Leveraging their powerful cross-modal understanding and reasoning capabilities, LLMs can significantly enhance the scene understanding ability of BEV systems, enabling more intelligent human–vehicle collaboration. For example, BEVInstructor integrates BEV representations into multi-modal large language models (MLLMs) to generate instructions, thereby enhancing semantic understanding of complex traffic scenarios. BEV-CLIP leverages the textual semantic understanding capabilities of LLMs to enable cross-modal retrieval from text to BEV features, effectively improving recognition and retrieval performance in long-tail scenarios. Talk2BEV combines large vision–language models (LVLMs) with BEV maps, enabling a unified system to perform multi-task collaboration. BEVDriver builds an LLM-based end-to-end closed-loop driving model, significantly enhancing the stability of autonomous driving systems in complex environments. In addition, BEV representations based on occupancy prediction are also rapidly evolving. Occupancy prediction provides complete 3D spatial information, enabling accurate understanding of complex environments.

**Challenges in Advanced BEV Perception** Despite the significant progress of BEV perception in areas such as multi-modal fusion and collaboration with large models, several critical challenges remain to be addressed. On the one hand, although diffusion models have shown remarkable performance in enhancing the quality of BEV representations, their high computational cost during inference and the tendency to produce blurry dynamic object generations limit their real-time capability and robustness. On the other hand, the integration of BEV perception with large language models is still in an exploratory phase. A major challenge lies in effectively aligning 3D spatial data with linguistic reasoning, which can lead to semantic inconsistencies and hinder comprehensive scene understanding. Moreover, occupancy prediction faces efficiency bottlenecks in achieving high-resolution mapping, and most existing methods are limited to single-frame prediction, making it difficult to model dynamic and changing environments. Incorporating multi-frame information to construct an occupancy flow is a key direction for enhancing temporal modeling capabilities and supporting motion understanding. At the same time, deploying BEV models on automotive embedded platforms (e.g.,NVIDIA Orin) still faces constraints in computing power, memory, and power consumption, along with strict requirements for low latency and high throughput in the perception pipeline. Achieving an efficient balance between accuracy and real-time performance thus remains a key challenge for practical deployment.

[^1]: ## 1\. Introduction

Environmental perception is a crucial component of autonomous driving systems, which provides digital driving environment information for the vehicle by perceiving the surrounding environment. The essence of the perception task is the 3D geometric reconstruction of the physical world. Current autonomous driving perception methods perform detection, segmentation, and similar tasks in either the perspective view (PV) or the bird’s-eye view (BEV). BEV has gained significant attention from academia and industry due to its advantages of absolute scale and unobstructed perspective.

The principle of BEV perception involves collecting data about the surrounding environment using onboard sensors, transforming the information into a consistent coordinate system, and ultimately creating an environmental model from a bird’s-eye view perspective. BEV perception, which provides comprehensive environmental information from a bird’s-eye view perspective and helps vehicles more accurately identify the positions of roads, vehicles, obstacles, and pedestrians, enables autonomous vehicles to make more precise and safer decisions. The technology of perception utilizes data from various sensors, including camera, LiDAR and Radar. Each sensor has its own characteristics and advantages, making it suitable for different scenarios.

BEV perception has significant advantages over traditional autonomous driving perception methods based on perspective view or front view. First, BEV avoids common issues in 2D tasks such as occlusion and scale variation. In the perspective view, objects may experience occlusion or scale changes due to perspective (e.g. appearing smaller when farther away), but these problems are significantly mitigated in bird’s-eye view. Secondly, BEV adopts a unified world coordinate system, enabling the integration of data from different sensors, temporal sequences, and spatial information. By converting image and LiDAR point cloud data into the BEV coordinate system for fusion, the vehicle’s perception module combines the semantic information from images with the geometric information from LiDAR. Spatiotemporal fusion further facilitates the perception and prediction of object-motion-states, thereby enhancing the accuracy of autonomous driving perception. Finally, BEV condenses intricate 3D sceneries into a 2D plane while preserving fundamental geometric relationships. It not only significantly decreases the computational complexity of subsequent tasks and promotes model performance, but also improves the efficiency of data annotation and model training. As a result, BEV representation significantly enhances the formulation and deployment of downstream activities, including detection, segmentation, and tracking.

As autonomous driving perception technology advances, various new techniques and methods have emerged. Related methods, concentrate on improving BEV perception to reduce computational costs and improve operational efficiency, while others emphasize integrating features from different sensors and perspectives to obtain more comprehensive data. ### 2.1. Task definition and related work

BEV denotes bird’s eye view, and BEV perception involves converting visual information captured by cameras or LiDAR into BEV features to perform perception tasks such as 3D object detection or map segmentation,. Conventional perspective view based approaches often address perception tasks such as 3D object detection, trajectory prediction, and instance segmentation in isolation, thereby substantially elevating computing expenses. Conversely, BEV perception consolidates tasks into a cohesive algorithmic framework, facilitating effective parallel processing and enhancing computing efficiency. Subsequently, we will present a comprehensive explanation of fundamental principles in BEV technology.

**Detection proposals** 3D object detection is one of the core tasks in BEV perception, which aims to accurately predict the position, size, and class of critical objects (such as vehicles, pedestrians, and obstacles) in three-dimensional space,,. The predicted information can effectively assist in vehicle route planning and enhance obstacle avoidance capabilities. The generation of object proposals play a vital role in improving both the efficiency and accuracy of 3D object detection,. There are two main methods for generating object proposals: grouping methods and window scoring methods.

The core idea of grouping method is to cluster similar elements, such as pixels, shapes, colors, and textures, from image or point cloud data to form target candidate regions. The approach demonstrates relatively high accuracy and strong robustness, but suffers from computational complexity and slow processing speed. Grouping based methods employs hierarchical image segmentation algorithms, which first divide the image into small regions and then progressively merge them into larger areas, finally forming a global segmentation of the image. To increase the number of candidate regions, grouping methods, uses multiple low-level segmentation or over-segmentation followed by a random merging process to diversify the hierarchy. During the random merging of image segmentation results, they typically rely on a set of key cues, including the geometric features of superpixels, color consistency within regions, texture patterns, luminance distribution, and edge contours. Grouping methods can be broadly classified into three categories based on the way candidate regions are generated: superpixels (SP), graph cut (GC), and edge contours (EC). CPMC, adopts a superpixel merging method similar to SelectiveSearch,, progressively combining similar superpixels to generate object proposals. RIGOR, use graph cut (GC) algorithms to partition images into multiple candidate regions. After ranking the features, it utilizes deduplication methods such as the Maximum Margin Relevance (MMR) metric to remove duplicate or similar regions, thereby ensuring the diversity of the candidate regions. Others, extracts edge or contour information from images to segment foreground and background areas, and further divides the image into multiple candidate regions, thereby generating object proposals.

Window scoring method evaluates each candidate window that is likely to contain the target object. In contrast to the grouping method, it exclusively outputs bounding boxes, offering higher computational efficiency. Additionally, window scoring based method provides greater flexibility in handling objects of varying sizes and shapes, but tends to produce candidate boxes with lower precision. Window scoring based method, involves sliding windows at various positions and scales, extracting the features of each window, and evaluating whether these windows are likely to contain the target using a scoring function. Scoring is performed using techniques such as SVM or edge matching, ultimately selecting high-quality candidate boxes.

**Semantic Segmentation** Semantic segmentation plays an indispensable role in BEV perception tasks, which is mainly divided into map segmentation and object segmentation. Map segmentation focuses on extracting static elements such as lane markings and roads to generate BEV maps for path planning,,. Object segmentation targets dynamic elements such as vehicles and pedestrians, providing support for behavior prediction and obstacle avoidance. BEV semantic segmentation not only enhances the depth and breadth of scene understanding but also directly impacts core tasks such as path planning, dynamic decision-making, and multi-modal fusion,. Traditional semantic segmentation is achieved using supervised learning algorithms. Firstly, clustering algorithms are employed to identify the neighborhood of each point for feature extraction, and traditional classifiers are used to perform local classification on each pixel. Then, a conditional random field model is introduced to account for contextual information, enabling global label smoothing and optimization. Finally, the process produces accurate semantic segmentation results. With the rapid development of deep learning, neural networks have demonstrated exceptional efficiency in feature extraction and classification tasks, significantly improving the performance and accuracy of semantic segmentation.

![](https://ars.els-cdn.com/content/image/1-s2.0-S1566253525007250-gr2.jpg)

Download: Download high-res image (507KB)

Semantic segmentation methods can be categorized into three types based on the form of input data: point-based methods, grid-based methods, and projection-based methods. Among the most representative point-based semantic segmentation methods are PointNet and PointNet++. PointNet employs shared MLPs to extract features for each point,utilizes pooling operations to aggregate global features, and performs point classification and segmentation by integrating global features with local features. Although PointNet effectively handles point cloud data and provides a global perspective, it may not fully capture local contextual information. To address the limitation, PointNet++ introduces the Set Abstraction module, which performs hierarchical clustering. The approach significantly improves the original PointNet’s ability to capture local contextual information during feature extraction. RandLA-Net significantly reduces the computational complexity of point cloud processing through a random sampling strategy. Compared to PointNet++, RandLA-Net demonstrates superior scalability and real-time processing capabilities when applied to large-scale point cloud data.

Grid-based methods convert point cloud data into a regular grid structure, which is processed using deep neural networks. FCPN employs a uniform sampling strategy for feature extraction and utilizes dense grid representations, which performs point cloud segmentation through a 3D convolutional network combined with a U-Net architecture. LatticeNet addresses the computational efficiency challenges of traditional voxel-based methods in processing large-scale point cloud data by introducing sparse convolution techniques. Additionally, it employs a multi-scale convolutional network to enhance segmentation accuracy.

The projection-based method involves projecting the 3D point cloud onto different 2D planes, performing semantic segmentation on the 2D images, and then projecting the segmentation results back to the 3D space. This process finally yields the point cloud segmentation results. RangeNet++ projects 3D point clouds onto RangeView images and leverages an hourglass network to extract multi-scale features, thereby improving segmentation accuracy. The segmentation results are subsequently back-projected into 3D space and further refined through post-processing. SqueezeSeg projects LiDAR point clouds onto RangeView images and employs a SqueezeNet-based network for semantic segmentation. Post-processing is performed using a conditional random field (CRF), and instance segmentation is achieved through clustering algorithms.

While BEV semantic segmentation plays an important role in scene understanding, it is fundamentally a 2.5D representation that mainly captures the layout of ground objects in the BEV plane. However, the absence of vertical information limits its ability to comprehensively represent 3D spatial semantics. To address this limitation, research has gradually expanded to semantic segmentation in 3D space, namely occupancy-level 3D semantic perception. Unlike BEV semantic segmentation, the OCC 3D semantic segmentation task predicts both the occupancy status and semantic label for each voxel within a 3D voxel grid. This makes it a higher-dimensional and more granular task for spatial scene understanding.

Currently, mainstream occupancy grid ground truth generation methods can be broadly categorized into two types: LiDAR point cloud-based occupancy annotation, and image or depth map-based back-projection supervision. OpenOccupancy adopts a static–dynamic separation strategy, where multi-frame LiDAR point clouds are fused to construct an initial occupancy grid. A pre-trained model is then used to predict a dense occupancy grid, and the two are finally fused to generate a complete 3D occupancy semantic ground truth. Similar to OpenOccupancy, SurroundOcc adopts a pipeline for generating dense occupancy semantic labels. The key difference lies in its use of a nearest-neighbor strategy to assign semantic labels to dense voxels. The approach enables fully automatic generation of 3D occupancy ground truth without the need for human annotation. OCC3D further addresses the handling of occluded regions and uncertain voxels, thereby enhancing the robustness and completeness of the generated labels. NeRF-LiDAR leverages simulation systems to learn more accurate 3D geometry and real-world details, enabling the generation of precise semantic labels. In addition, some methods, attempt to alleviate the difficulty of obtaining occupancy labels by employing a back-projection supervision strategy. Specifically, the predicted occupancy grids are projected into 2D depth maps, which are then supervised using ground-truth depth data. OccNet enhances occupancy and semantic prediction accuracy by leveraging multi-modal fusion that combines the spatial structural awareness inherent in BEV representations with the rich semantic cues provided by image features. In summary, OCC 3D semantic segmentation enables more comprehensive scene understanding and facilitates downstream tasks by building a full 3D semantic representation of complex environments.

**Multi-Sensor Fusion** Multi-sensor fusion is a critical component of BEV perception technology. By integrating the strengths of data from various sensors, BEV perception can construct a more comprehensive representation of the scene,,. In autonomous driving systems, vehicles are typically equipped with multiple sensors, such as cameras, LiDAR and Radar, as well as RGB-D cameras and infrared cameras that have been introduced in certain research studies. Each with distinct characteristics and advantages,while this study primarily focuses on the three most widely used and extensively studied sensors in autonomous driving scenarios: cameras, LiDAR and Radar. Cameras provide high-resolution RGB images containing rich color and texture information, which are beneficial for object recognition and classification. However, cameras cannot directly acquire depth information, making precise object localization challenging. LiDAR sensors offer significant advantages in providing high-precision depth information and perceiving environmental structures. However, adverse weather conditions can affect their performance. Additionally, LiDAR point cloud data is relatively sparse and lacks rich color and texture information. Radar excels in detecting objects at long ranges and demonstrates strong anti-interference capabilities, allowing it to operate effectively under complex weather conditions. However, Radar has relatively low perception accuracy, and its data is sparse, lacking the ability to provide detailed information on object shapes and textures. Fusing data from different modalities enables the complementary strengths of various sensors to be leveraged, thereby capturing and utilizing environmental information more comprehensively,,.

In addition, the fusion of LiDAR, Radar, RGB cameras, as well as RGB-D cameras and infrared cameras represents a promising direction for further exploration. Nevertheless, existing studies in this area remain relatively limited. RGB-D cameras are capable of generating dense depth maps, excelling in near-field scenarios, and providing significant value for object detection, localization, and navigation. However, the sensing range is limited to only a few meters and they may be affected by low-texture areas and challenging lighting conditions. In contrast, infrared cameras can detect temperature differences between objects and their surroundings under low-light or low-visibility conditions (e.g., at night or in fog), and convert these differences into thermal images. However, infrared cameras typically have lower resolution than RGB cameras and are more expensive. As a result, they have not yet been widely adopted and remain primarily in the experimental research phase.

Currently, multi-sensor fusion strategies can be broadly categorized into two main types: perception-level fusion and decision-level fusion. Perception-level fusion involves combining raw data or intermediate features from multiple sensors at the perception stage of the model, aiming to achieve more accurate and robust perception outcomes. Perception-level fusion can be classified into early fusion and intermediate fusion, depending on the stage at which data from different sensors are integrated. The principles of these fusion stages are illustrated in.

In early fusion, the raw data collected from different sensors are first synchronized and then directly fused before being fed into the perception model. VPFNet, enhances the expressive power of LiDAR point clouds by incorporating semantic information from images during the input stage. Data-level fusion retains the most information, but the heterogeneity between camera and LiDAR data leads to challenges such as coordinate transformation difficulties and high computational overhead, making it complex to implement. Intermediate fusion, also referred to as feature-level fusion, integrates features extracted independently from LiDAR and images during the feature extraction stage. Wang proposed a method that extracts features from both LiDAR point clouds and images, followed by deep-level fusion to effectively integrate complementary information from the two modalities, leading to a significant improvement in detection performance. Feature-level fusion is widely adopted in BEV perception due to its advantages such as minimal data loss and low computational overhead.

Late fusion, also known as decision-level fusion, refers to the strategy where each sensor independently performs object detection, and the results are subsequently fused at the decision level. MV3D projects LiDAR point clouds onto the BEV map and utilizes a Region Proposal Network (RPN) to generate candidate object regions. SE-SSD is proposed based on MV3D, generating object proposals from BEV maps and feature maps extracted from RGB images. TransFusion generates a set of image proposals and projects their features into the 3D frustum space for object detection. The type of proposal-level fusion is typically applied in multi-stage detection pipelines and is inherently object-centric, which limits its extensibility to other tasks such as BEV map segmentation,.

### 2.3. Evaluation metrics

**Mean Average Precision (mAP)** is a key metric for evaluating object detection tasks in BEV perception. In traditional object detection, Average Precision (AP) is determined by calculating the Intersection over Union (IoU) between predicted boxes and ground truth boxes. However, in the BEV perspective, the complexity of positional and geometric relationships in three-dimensional space makes IoU computation particularly challenging. To address this challenge, researchers have adopted the distance between the center of the detection box and the center of the ground-truth box as a substitute for IoU, thereby reducing the error caused by bounding box offset. Typically, a detection distance threshold is set, and the detection is considered correct when the detected distance is less than the given threshold. By setting a series of confidence thresholds, corresponding precision and recall values can be calculated. These values are used to plot the Precision–Recall (PR) curve, and the AP is computed by integrating the area under the PR curve. (1) $A P = \int_{0}^{1} \text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right) d r$

$\text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right)$ denotes the precision at a given recall rate $r$. Finally, the mean Average Precision (mAP) is obtained by averaging the AP values calculated across all thresholds. In addition, Waymo Open proposed the AP Hungarian algorithm and incorporated directional weighting into the AP metric, introducing the APH metric to enhance evaluation precision. KITTI uses the PASCAL standard to evaluate object detection performance in the BEV perspective.

**NuScenes Detection Score (NDS)** is a comprehensive evaluation metric proposed in the nuScenes dataset. This evaluation mechanism integrates mAP with multiple attribute error metrics — mean Translation Error (mATE), mean Scale Error (mASE), mean Orientation Error (mAOE), mean Velocity Error (mAVE), and mean Attribute Error (mAAE) — to prevent models from over-optimizing a single metric while neglecting other critical factors. As shown in Equ,, the comprehensive metric enables simultaneous evaluation of detection accuracy, geometric attributes, and dynamic properties, making it more aligned with the requirements of autonomous driving (2) $\text{TP}_{\text{score}} = max \left(\right. 1 - \text{TP}_{\text{error}} , 0 . 0 \left.\right)$ (3) $\text{NDS} = \frac{5 \cdot \text{mAP} + \sum_{i = 1}^{5} \text{TP}_{\text{score}}^{i}}{10}$

**Mean Intersection over Union (mIOU)** is a standard metric for evaluating semantic segmentation, frequently applied to assess the performance of 3D occupancy prediction models. Similar to 2D pixel-level semantic segmentation, mIoU measures the degree of overlap between the predicted results and the ground truth labels. mIoU effectively reflects the model’s accuracy in recognizing various objects within a scene. The formula for mIoU is as follows: (4) $\text{mIoU} = \frac{1}{C} \times \sum_{i = 1}^{c} \frac{T P_{i}}{T P_{i} + F P_{i} + F N_{i}}$

$C$ denotes the number of semantic classes, and $T P_{i}$, $F P_{i}$, and $F N_{i}$ represent the true positives, false positives, and false negatives for class $i$, respectively. When semantic labels are not considered, $\text{IoU}$ can also serve as an evaluation metric to measure the accuracy of occupancy prediction in space. (5) $\text{IoU} = \frac{T P}{T P + F P + F N}$

**LET-3D-APL** is a metric specifically designed for evaluating 3D object detection, which emphasizes tolerance and weighting of longitudinal errors. This metric allows predicted boxes to exhibit longitudinal localization errors within a given tolerance range and effectively mitigates such errors by introducing longitudinal affinity to weight the precision. The calculation formula is as follows: (6) $\text{LET}-\text{3D}-\text{APL} = \int_{0}^{1} \text{Prec}_{L} \left(\right. r \left.\right) d r = \int_{0}^{1} \overset{̄}{a}_{l} \cdot \text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right) d r$

$\text{Prec}_{L} \left(\right. r \left.\right)$ is the longitudinal affinity-weighted precision, $\text{Prec} \left(\right. r \left.\right)$ is the precision at recall rate $r$, and $\overset{̄}{a}_{l}$ is the average longitudinal affinity.

### 3.1. Camera-centric BEV perception

Camera-only perception has garnered significant attention from both academia and industry. Compared to perception solutions that rely on high-precision LiDAR, camera-only BEV perception systems offer a more cost-effective alternative while maintaining competitive performance. However, the approach presents an inherent challenge:the imaging process of cameras results in the loss of partial 3D information, which prevents the acquisition of accurate depth information and thus hinders precise target localization. As shown in, camera-only 3D perception framework mainly consists of three components:the image-view encoder is primarily used to process the input RGB images and extract features;the view transformation module converts image features into BEV features;the BEV decoder further decodes the transformed features. Finally, the generated BEV features are applied to specific tasks. The view transformation module is crucial in pure vision-based BEV perception, as it lifts 2D features into 3D space generating BEV features for downstream tasks such as object detection and semantic segmentation,.

View transformation methodologies can be categorized into three principal approaches. The first method is the ’2D–3D methods’, which predicts the depth distribution of pixels from 2D image features and then ‘lifts’ these 2D features into 3D space based on the estimated depth information. The second method is the ’3D-2D methods’, which populates the corresponding 3D voxel grid with 2D image features based on constraints from the camera’s intrinsic and extrinsic parameters, along with depth estimation. The third category employs an ’end-to-end network architecture,’ which discards geometric prior constraints and directly leverages BEV-annotated data to train deep neural networks, thereby establishing an implicit mapping between image pixels and BEV grids.

#### 3.1.1. 2D-to-3D view transformation

The 2D–3D method involves predicting the depth position of each pixel based on 2D image features. By leveraging depth information, the related method enables the understanding of an object’s position in 3D space, thereby facilitating the transformation from 2D images to 3D space. LSS is a typical 2D–3D transformation method. The approach ‘lifts’ 2D image features into the 3D space based on the predicted depth information, then ‘projects’ the generated 3D point cloud features onto the rasterized BEV grid to achieve the perspective transformation. The specific conversion process is as follows: First, the discrete depth probability distribution $\alpha_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right)$ is predicted at each pixel (u, v). Then, $\alpha_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right)$ is multiplied with the 2D image features C(u, v) to obtain the voxel features at each depth layer. (7) $\mathbf{c}_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right) = \alpha_{d} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right) \cdot \mathbf{C} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right)$ Subsequently, these features are geometrically transformed $M_{\text{trans}}$ into the 3D voxel coordinate system based on the camera intrinsics $\mathbf{K}$ and extrinsics $\left[\right. \mathbf{R} \left|\right. \mathbf{T} \left]\right.$, resulting in 3D spatial features $\mathcal{F}_{3 D} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right)$. (8) $\mathcal{F}_{\text{3D}} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right) = M_{\text{trans}} \left(\right. \mathcal{F}_{\text{2D}} \left(\right. u , v \left.\right) , \mathbf{K} , \left[\right. \mathbf{R} \mid \mathbf{T} \left]\right. \left.\right)$ Finally, the generated 3D spatial features $\mathcal{F}_{3 D} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right)$ are projected along the height axis to obtain the BEV representation $F_{\text{BEV}} \left(\right. x , y \left.\right)$. (9) $F_{\text{BEV}} \left(\right. x , y \left.\right) = \underset{z}{\sum} F_{3 D} \left(\right. x , y , z \left.\right)$

However, this process inevitably introduces depth estimation errors, which mainly stem from inaccurate predictions of depth probability distributions, quantization errors caused by depth discretization, and depth discontinuities. Specifically, inaccurate depth distribution prediction leads to projection deviations in the BEV feature space; discretizing depth into a fixed number of bins introduces quantization errors; and the instability between discrete depth bins and softmax outputs results in spatial discontinuities of the 3D projected points, giving rise to the phenomenon of “depth jumping”. Such errors directly affect the accuracy of BEV representations, thereby degrading the performance and robustness of downstream tasks such as detection and segmentation. To address this issue, subsequent studies based on the LSS framework have proposed various methods to mitigate these errors.

Based on the LSS paradigm, BEVDet employs a similar architecture that predicts depth through a classification-based approach and significantly improves depth prediction accuracy by supervising depth estimation with LiDAR reference data. Furthermore, the paper proposes a scale non-maximum suppression(NMS) method that effectively addresses redundancy issues in small object detection, offering novel insights for 3D object detection. Although the depth estimation of LSS paradigm struggles to meet high-precision 3D detection requirements in certain scenarios, the method can obtain explicit BEV features for downstream tasks. BEVDepth significantly enhances depth estimation accuracy through explicit depth supervision and further optimizes predictions by incorporating a depth refinement module.

MambaOcc builds upon the LSS paradigm for view transformation, which extracts 2D image features through an image backbone network and generates discrete depth values for each pixel. The key distinction between LSS and MambaOcc lies in the latter’s use of a voxel pooling method to directly aggregate depth prediction results, thus simplifying the depth feature fusion strategy. To simplify model architectures and improve computational efficiency, some approaches, employ bilinear sampling to lift image features into 3D voxel space, where the features implicitly encode depth-related information. While processing multi-view images, LSS may not fully exploit the correlations between adjacent surround-view images, resulting in fragmented extraction of image features. To address these limitations, GraphBEV introduces a LocalAlign module that enhances depth feature perception through graph matching with neighboring context, improving the accuracy of feature transformation.

#### 3.1.2. 3D-to-2D view transformation

In addition to the explicit depth distribution estimation strategies used in LSS and BEVDet, another approach refers to ’3D-2D methods’, which projects 2D image features into corresponding voxels through geometric mapping. Based on depth estimation and camera intrinsics parameters, each voxel aggregates image features from its projected regions on the image plane. OFT represents a representative 3D-2D method, which constructs a uniformly distributed 3D voxel grid, accumulating image features from the projected regions. The orthographic BEV feature map is then generated by flattening voxel features in the height direction. When compressing the voxel grid in the height dimension, a weighted averaging approach is typically adopted to aggregate information from multiple height layers into a single BEV feature to reduce computational load. This method not only decreases the computational cost of depth estimation but also fully utilizes depth information in the scene.

Recently, the combination of 3D-2D projection and neural network has become increasingly popular. Because neural network can efficiently extract and process image features, especially when dealing with complex scenes and dynamic environments. For example, the cross-attention mechanism in Transformer can adaptively focus on key regions in 3D space and associate these key points with 2D image features across modalities, which significantly enhances the robustness of features in complex dynamic scenes. Some approaches,, utilize the intrinsic and extrinsic camera matrices to project a set of 3D query points onto the planes of multi-view images and aggregate image features to achieve 3D object detection. Additionally, researchers have designed a projected cross-attention mechanism to address the issues of insufficient utilization of geometric cues and loss of cross-view object information in existing methods. BEVFormer employs the cross-attention mechanism to align 3D query points with 2D image features, achieving efficient 3D-2D view transformation. Based on BEVFormer, BEVFormerV2 introduces a perspective supervision mechanism. By adding auxiliary detection heads to the image backbone network to supervise multi-view images, it significantly enhances the geometric consistency of BEV features. BEVMamba introduces Mamba for temporal modeling, thereby improving upon the BEVFormer paradigm that relies on attention mechanisms for temporal modeling. This improvement strategy effectively addresses the issue of low efficiency when attention mechanisms handle long sequences. Other related methods,, employ convolutional networks to extract features from multi-view images and utilize projective network modules to implicitly learn 3D-to −2D geometric correspondences for generating robust BEV representations

However, BEVFormer and some networks on depth information prediction consume substantial computational resources. To enhance computational efficiency and network performance, Fast-BEV introduces a lightweight and deployment-friendly view transformation. The related method obtains the BEV representation by projecting multi-view 2D image features along camera rays onto a 3D grid, which significantly enhances computational efficiency. Furthermore, MetaBEV also introduces a cross-modal attention mechanism to dynamically adjust the feature fusion strategy, addressing the issue of sensor temporal validity and ensuring high-quality BEV feature generation even in cases of sensor damage or absence.

#### 3.1.3. Pure network-based methods for BEV generation

Both ’2D-to −3D methods’ and ’3D-to −2D methods’ rely on the inherited inductive bias in geometric projection, which makes the geometric models dependent on complex parameter tuning and hinders large-scale deployment. In comparison, pure network-based approaches tend to utilize multi-layer perceptron mechanism,, or Transformer,, architecture to implicitly represent the mapping from camera views to the bird’s-eye-view. These architectures enable end-to-end training and eliminate the dependency on explicit depth estimation. MLP serves as a mapping function, accepting data from different sensors as input and producing corresponding BEV outputs, thereby facilitating efficient transformations from PV to BEV. VPN utilizes MLP for processing multi-camera views to generate bird’s-eye-view. And the related method supports the accumulation of information over multiple time steps, enhancing the stability and robustness of the map. PON employs the concept of column-wise view transformation, firstly extracting multi-scale image features from a feature pyramid. Subsequently, PON folds features along the vertical direction through MLP and expands them along the depth axis, ultimately accomplishing the view transformation as shown in. On the basis of PON, BEV-LANEDET has designed a lightweight view transformation module based on MLP, which converts multi-scale front-view feature maps into BEV representations, achieving more accurate and efficient 3D lane detection. VectorMapNet introduces a two-layer MLP that learns the projection relationship between input embeddings and outputs, enabling the prediction of keypoint locations and category labels. The method lays a solid foundation for the generation of vectorized maps.

While MLP has achieved remarkable results in the view transformation from PV to BEV, it faces challenges in effectively handling the global positional relationships among multiple views. In contrast, the attention mechanism in the Transformer architecture enables cross-view learning, which facilitates a better understanding of global positional information. Furthermore, the robust data dependency and scalability of the Transformer allows them to learn more flexible feature representations, enabling the handling of more complex scenes and adaptation to varying task requirements. CVT embeds positional information through the internal and external calibration of cameras and introduces a cross-view attention mechanism to achieve the projection from camera view to BEV. DETR3D employs a Transformer architecture to enhance BEV features for better predictions. Bev-locator projects multi-view image features into the BEV space. Additionally, the method introduces a cross-modal Transformer module, which infers the ego pose by computing self-attention and cross-attention. DiffBEV proposes an end-to-end framework that utilizes a cross-attention module to merge BEV features. However, the Transformer-based approaches adopt unidirectional and posterior interaction mechanisms, which pose challenges for the transformation from perspective view to bird’s eye view. To address this issue, BAEFormer proposes a bi-directional and early interaction transformers framework, which further enhances the algorithm’s performance by downsampling multiview image features. The implicit methods no longer rely on explicit geometric transformations for the projection from PV to BEV, but instead focus more on training neural networks with large amounts of data to achieve efficient view transformation.

#### 3.2.1. Voxel-based methods

Voxel-based methods discretize 3D point cloud data into voxels, thus providing an efficient and structured representation for 3D scene understanding.VoxelNet discretizes the point cloud data using voxel methods, and then compresses the extracted voxel features into the BEV space to perform detection and segmentation tasks. VoxelNeXt adopts the core concept of VoxelNet, converting point cloud features into BEV representations after feature extraction and ultimately performing 3D detection tasks. As illustrated in, the primary distinction between VoxelNeXt and VoxelNet lies in their feature transformation strategies: VoxelNet utilizes max-pooling operations to compress 3D voxel features into BEV space, necessitating intermediate dense tensor conversions and NMS post-processing. In contrast, VoxelNeXt adopts a fully sparse convolutional approach that directly bridges 3D sparse features to BEV representations through parameterized geometric projection, eliminating the need for dense tensor transformations and NMS stages. The architectural innovation significantly reduces computational complexity while maintaining competitive accuracy.

Existing methods,, predominantly focus on single-frame point cloud data, neglecting the spatiotemporal information inherent in sequential LiDAR point clouds. To fully exploit the valuable temporal cues in time-series data, InsMos takes a sequence of LiDAR point clouds as input and incorporates temporal information through 4D voxel quantization. The framework subsequently leverages sparse convolution to extract motion-aware features from these 4D voxels for instance detection. Park proposes a 3D object detection framework, which enriches the representation of voxel features by designing a temporal voxel encoder to capture the temporal relationships between point sets within voxels. The method then analyzes discrepancies between consecutive BEV feature maps to extract motion information, which is integrated into the current BEV features to achieve more accurate 3D object detection. KAN-RCBEVDepth aligns point cloud features from multiple temporal frames to the current coordinate system, enhancing the accuracy of feature representation. Furthermore, the paper proposes an optimized voxel pooling method that efficiently aggregates multi-modal features into the BEV and leverages CUDA thread-based parallel processing of voxels, significantly improving computational efficiency.

In 3D object detection tasks, LiDAR point clouds exhibit an uneven distribution in 3D space, resulting in significant disparities in their contributions to object detection across different spatial regions. To address the non-uniformity of point cloud distributions, Focal Sparse Conv first predicts critical regions of importance in 3D space and then extracts features using sparse convolution, substantially enhancing the capability of convolutional networks. Building upon Focal Sparse Conv, MDRNet proposes a point cloud detection network based on a multi-stage feature dimensionality reduction strategy. During feature transformation, the network dynamically focuses on regions with high data contribution, thereby mitigating information loss and reducing computational overhead. SlENet proposes a spatial information enhancement network that demonstrates robustness to density-imbalanced point clouds, achieving precise 3D object detection.

#### 3.2.2. Pillar-based methods

The core of the Pillar-based method lies in organizing sparse LiDAR point clouds into regular pillar structures, thereby streamlining the feature extraction pipeline. Specifically, the point cloud data is first discretized into a 2D grid, and then the extracted pillar features are mapped into a pseudo-image to generate BEV features. Compared to voxel-based methods, pillar-based methods significantly reduce computational resource consumption, achieving higher computational efficiency and real-time performance. PointPillars pioneered a pillar-based feature extraction paradigm, which partitions point clouds into vertical pillar structures. This approach learns latent representations through pseudo-image encoding and executes downstream perception tasks. However, PointPillars and other pillar-based methods,, suffer from limited pillar feature encoding capabilities, struggling to detect distant or small-sized objects. To enhance small object detection performance, pillarNet designs a more powerful encoding network for pillar feature learning and incorporates multi-scale feature fusion, thus better capturing local geometric details. MVF introduces an end-to-end multi-view fusion algorithm that integrates bird’s-eye view and perspective-view features to learn more effective pillar representations, enhancing 3D object detection performance. Fastpillars introduces a lightweight max-and-attention pillar encoding module that enhances feature representation for small 3D objects by aggregating critical local geometric patterns. Additionally, the paper designs a deployment-friendly pillar-based 3D detector capable of achieving high-performance and low-latency object detection without relying on sparse convolutions.

To further advance real-time performance, FlatFormer designs a flattened window attention mechanism, effectively addressing the computational bottlenecks of point cloud Transformers when processing 3D point clouds and narrowing the latency gap. PointBeV adopts an efficient two-stage sparse computation strategy, which significantly reduces computational redundancy while maintaining accuracy, and enables fast adaptation to different platforms through flexible memory control. MotionBEV converts 3D LiDAR scans into 2D polar BEV representations, significantly boosting computational efficiency. The method also adaptively fuse spatiotemporal information from appearance and motion features, thereby enhancing the accuracy of temporal prediction. CenterPoint employs a network architecture similar to PointPillars to construct a representation of the input point cloud, then flattens these features into the BEV feature map for object detection. By representing 3D objects as points, it significantly simplifies target recognition and further improves real-time performance.

Additionally, Pillar-od proposes a pillar-based method to address anchor-induced imbalance issues. By integrating cylindrical projection into multi-view feature learning and predicting bounding box parameters per pillar rather than per point, this approach optimizes prediction accuracy and enhances detection performance. Both voxel-based and pillar-based methods partition 3D space into discrete regions, each containing subsets of point cloud data. Voxel-based approaches discretize irregular point clouds into regular voxel grids, enabling direct application of 3D convolutional neural networks for feature extraction. However, this process often introduces numerous empty voxels, resulting in inefficient computational resource utilization. In contrast, pillar-based methods offer greater flexibility by organizing point clouds into vertical pillar structures. Each pillar encapsulates point cloud information within a defined spatial range. And the pillar dimensions can be adaptively adjusted based on task requirements, thereby significantly improving computational efficiency.

#### 3.3.1. Camera–LiDAR fusion

Multi-sensor fusion enables complementary advantages across different sensing modalities, allowing systems to holistically capture environmental information. Camera data provides dense color and texture information but lacks accurate depth perception, whereas LiDAR delivers precise depth measurements information at the cost of sparse data density and limited perception range. Multi-modal fusion can preserve the semantic information of images and the geometric information of LiDAR, obtaining more comprehensive environmental information. BEVFusion represents a representative Camera–LiDAR fusion approach, which employs a separate dual-branch framework to generate BEV representations from LiDAR and camera data respectively as shown in. The approach fuses these multi-modal feature maps through cross-view interaction, and performs downstream perception tasks including object detection and map segmentation. Based on BEVFusion, BEVFusion4D designs a LiDAR-guided view transformation module, which leverages the spatial cues from LiDAR to more accurately extract semantic information from images, thereby obtaining more effective BEV representations. Additionally, BEVFusion4D utilizes temporal information to aggregate BEV features from multiple historical frames, achieving more accurate perception.

Most related methods still face the challenge of inaccurate depth estimation in areas with significant depth changes. To alleviate the depth jump problem, BEVDepth aligns the depth estimates with the projected depth of the point cloud through minimum pooling and one-hot operations. EA-LSS proposes a edge-aware LSS framework, which enhances the fine-grained supervision of depth by adjusting the size of the predicted depth map and the ground-truth depth map, enabling the model to better adapt to rapid depth changes between objects. Current 3D perception methods mainly use modality-specific encoders to extract features from different sensors and then transform them into a unified BEV space. However, this process leads to additional computational overhead. To reduce the consumption of computational resources, FUTR3D designs a modality-agnostic feature sampler that can sample and aggregate features from different sensors, providing a low-cost and high-performance perception system for autonomous driving. UniTR introduces a modality-agnostic transformer encoder, which simplifies the network architecture by processing different modality feature representations in parallel and enabling automatic cross-modal interaction. Although multi-sensor fusion is increasingly gaining attention in this field, it is still affected by poor image conditions. To address this issue, TransFusion proposes a robust Camera–LiDAR fusion method that uses the attention mechanism of the Transformer to adaptively extract image information, generating a stable and effective fusion strategy. CMT proposes a hybrid network based on the Transformer to capture long-range dependencies in images, improving the overall accuracy of the model.

Although current multi-modal BEV perception methods haveachieved remarkable results on standard datasets such as nuScenes and KITTI, they still face numerous challenges in real-world deployment. First, sensor failures may cause incomplete or distorted perception information; therefore, systems need to design effective fault detection and redundancy mechanisms to enhance robustness. Second, the high costs of high-performance sensors and computing platforms limit large-scale deployment, making low-cost sensor alternatives and optimized resource allocation key directions for practical applications. Additionally, variations in sensor installation positions, environmental changes, and inconsistent data distributions pose challenges to system stability and generalization performance. To enable BEV perception systems to generalize in diverse scenarios, transfer techniques have become essential, mainly encompassing three aspects: sensor calibration, data annotation, and model fine-tuning.

In terms of calibration, CalibRBEV proposed an automatic multi-camera calibration method based on inverse BEV representations. By reconstructing BEV features, the method inversely infers the intrinsic and extrinsic parameters of cameras, thereby achieving high-precision automatic calibration. Moreover, OpenCalib, as a multi-sensor calibration toolbox, offers a variety of automatic and online calibration methods to support calibration requirements across different application scenarios, significantly advancing the practical deployment of BEV perception systems. Calibration techniques ensure that sensor data can still be accurately projected onto the BEV plane in new environments, alleviating geometric deviations caused by differences in physical sensor installation.

In terms of annotation, high-quality data labeling is fundamental for training and evaluating BEV perception models. However, traditional 3D annotation methods struggle to capture temporal continuity and object motion trajectories in dynamic scenes. To address this, 4D annotation techniques have emerged, introducing the time dimension on top of 3D spatial labeling, enabling a more comprehensive description of object trajectories and behavioral changes. Auto4D employs an automated approach to generate high-quality 4D annotations, significantly reducing the cost of manual labeling and enhancing the deployability and adaptability of BEV perception models across diverse scenarios.

Regarding model fine-tuning, BEV perception systems often need to adapt to different scenarios and tasks. However, directly training models in new environments can face challenges such as insufficient data and high annotation costs. To address this, researchers have proposed a BEV perception network fine-tuning method based on visual 2D semantic perception. The approach leverages mature 2D perception techniques to reduce reliance on costly BEV annotation data, significantly improving the model’s generalization ability in new scenarios. Additionally, BEVGPT enhances the adaptability of BEV models by performing online fine-tuning of pre-trained models within autonomous driving simulation environments, enabling dynamic prediction of future trajectories.

#### 3.3.2. Temporal information fusion

Temporal information plays a significant role in predicting the motion state of objects and detecting occluded objects. By integrating data from multiple historical frames, temporal fusion compensates for the limitations of single-frame perception and significantly improves the accuracy of perception algorithms,. As illustrated in, BEVFormer employs predefined grid-shaped BEV queries to interact with spatiotemporal information, generating a unified BEV representation. Additionally, the approach introduces a temporal self-attention module to fuse historical BEV information, further enhancing the BEV features. TempBEV, proposes an effective mechanism for fusing data from different views. The mechanism aggregates temporal information from both the image latent space and the BEV latent space and leverages optical flow estimation for temporal stereo encoding, achieving higher detection accuracy and robustness. BEVFormer and some LSS-based methods primarily rely on transforming the image view into a Cartesian BEV representation. These methods overlook the non-uniform distribution of image information and find it hard to leverage view symmetry.

To address these limitations, PolarDETR proposes replacing Cartesian BEV representations with polar BEV representations. Under this polar coordinate framework, PolarDETR projects the sampling anchor points of the current frame to previous frames to retrieve historical image features. Subsequently, the method aggregates features from the current frame and past frames to update the query embeddings. The temporal fusion mechanism enables more effective capture of dynamic object cues for enhanced motion-aware perception. Based on the PolarDETR paradigm, PolarBEVDet leverages ego-motion to align historical features with the current timestamp for temporal fusion, achieving superior performance in multi-view 3D object detection tasks.

MGTANet designs a 3D object detection architecture, which introduces short-term motion-aware voxel feature encoding to obtain BEV feature maps. Additionally, the method proposes a long-term feature aggregation method to guide BEV feature enhancement, constructing complementary short-term and long-term temporal fusion. BEVDepth, employs a multi-frame fusion mechanism that aligns the coordinates of the view frustum features from different frames with the current ego-coordinate system, eliminating the influence of ego-motion. Such alignment enables the model to handle dynamic scenes more accurately.

#### 3.3.3. Camera–radar fusion

While multi-modal sensor fusion combining LiDAR and cameras has become the gold standard for high-performance environmental perception systems, the integration of Radar and cameras is rapidly emerging as a highly promising alternative solution,. Fusing cameras with low-cost Radar can provide accurate long-range measurements and ensure reliable operation in various environments. illustrates the general process of fusing point cloud data with images. Therefore, Camera–Radar fusion offers significant advantages in terms of cost-effectiveness and environmental adaptability. Radar sensors are widely used in automotive systems. However, issues such as Radar sparsity, the lack of height information, and measurement noise have led to the overlook of Radar’s potential in 3D detection and tracking,,. To address these issues, CR3DT proposes a Camera–Radar multi-modal fusion model based on the BEVDet architecture. The model enhances 3D object detection and tracking performance by leveraging Radar’s spatial localization and velocity perception capabilities. SparseFusion3D employs a dual-branch network to separately process Radar and image features, followed by feature-level fusion. This architecture effectively mitigates the adverse effects of Radar sparsity and noise on modality association. In addition, the paper introduces a multi-scale point cloud projection module to compensate for point cloud offset issues. HVDetFusion utilizes prior information about different target locations to filter out false positives in the raw Radar data, identifying and eliminating noise points in the Radar point clouds. As a result, target detection accuracy and robustness are significantly enhanced.

To avoid information loss issues in traditional BEV fusion, CRN leverages sparse but accurate Radar points to transform image features into BEV features. And the approach employs multi-modal deformable attention to further aggregate these BEV features, generating semantically rich and spatially accurate BEV feature maps. CRAFT converts image coordinates to the polar coordinate system of the Radar to achieve coordinate alignment. Subsequently, the approach adaptively fuses spatiotemporal information through a cross-attention mechanism, enhancing the representational capacity of cross-modal features. Camera–Radar fusion is favored for its exceptional sensing capabilities and cost-effectiveness, but the substantial modality differences pose significant challenges to effective cross-modal integration. RCBEVDet++ converts sparse Radar points into dense BEV features and then utilizes a deformable attention mechanism for feature alignment. Additionally, the paper incorporates both channel and spatial fusion layers for multi-modal fusion, significantly enhancing the performance of 3D object detection. TransCAR achieves a soft association between Radar features and visual update queries through the cross-attention layer in the transformer decoder. The soft association mechanism can dynamically learn the matching relationships between features, enhancing the effectiveness of multi-modal fusion.

### 3.4. BEV enhancement techniques

#### 3.4.1. Prior information filtering

In the field of autonomous driving perception, multi-sensor fusion is crucial for achieving accurate environmental perception. But data from different sensors vary in terms of accuracy, timestamps, perspectives, and may contain noise in the raw data. These factors may interfere with subsequent feature extraction and model prediction. To ensure data consistency and accuracy, preprocessing through pre-information filtering is necessary to reduce the interference of invalid data on BEV feature projection. However, existing methods are susceptible to inaccuracies in sensor calibration, leading to misalignment between LiDAR and camera BEV features. Some methods employ a cross-attention mechanism to allow features from different modalities to guide each other for alignment, mitigating the feature misalignment issue caused by projection matrix errors between LiDAR and cameras. ContrastAlign proposes utilizing contrastive learning to enhance the alignment of heterogeneous modalities, improving the robustness of the fusion process. Building upon ContrastAlign, GraphBEV introduces the LocalAlign module and the GlobalAlign module. The LocalAlign module primarily relies on graph matching and adopts locally aware depth features to enrich contextual depth feature learning. The GlobalAlign module employs learnable offsets to achieve global multi-modal feature alignment. Other methods add a deformable alignment layer in the multi-modal fusion network, using the flexibility of deformable convolution to dynamically compensate for calibration offsets.

Interferences in complex road environments may lead to the loss or incompleteness of key frames, impacting the overall perception capability. Against this background, Co-Fix3D employs an attention mechanism to filter out important features, further enhancing the overall perception capability. To maintain efficient computation while aggregating contextual information, SparseDet adopts a sparse sampling strategy, which selectively aggregates voxel features within regions of interest. The related approach not only addresses the issue of insufficient information representation in traditional sparse detection methods but also significantly enhances inference speed. RoadBEV aligns and calibrates left and right images to ensure consistency between views, reducing noise caused by sensor calibration errors and perspective differences. On the other hand, RopeBEV introduces the region of interest mask module, which filters out invalid perception areas by defining regions of interest. The filtering process ensures that the input feature set is more focused on key features relevant to the object detection task, thus enhancing the efficiency and accuracy of subsequent processing. This process enhances the performance of 3D object detection.

#### 3.4.2. Enhancement of intermediate layer features

The enhancement of intermediate layer features aims to optimize feature representation to address the challenges of viewpoint changes and complex scenes, thereby improving the model’s perception performance in the BEV space. Specific enhancement techniques include: multi-modal feature fusion to enhance the complementarity of modal information, the introduction of attention mechanisms to focus on key areas and suppress redundant information, and feature alignment optimization to address geometric distortions caused by perspective differences. Furthermore, diffusion models have emerged as a powerful feature enhancement approach due to their strengths in feature reconstruction and denoising. BEVWorld employs a noise-based diffusion process within the BEV latent space to iteratively optimize feature distributions, gradually improving the sharpness of feature representations and the accuracy of downstream predictions. BEVDiffuser utilizes real-world object layouts as guidance to effectively denoise the BEV feature maps. To further enhance semantic granularity, DiffBEV introduces a conditional diffusion model that guides coarse BEV features toward a more fine-grained semantic space. In addition, DifFUSER models multi-modal fusion as a diffusion process, enabling automatic feature completion under sensor failure or degradation, which significantly enhances the robustness and adaptability of the fusion results.

Although multi-modal fusion is widely favored in the field of autonomous driving, existing fusion methods are highly susceptible to poor image conditions. These impacts can lead insufficient robustness of multi-modal fusion when facing complex environmental conditions. TransFusion proposes a robust LiDAR–camera fusion strategy that tackles poor image quality through a soft association mechanism. Additionally, the method utilizes an attention mechanism to adaptively extract target information from images, achieving robust and effective fusion results. To more effectively handle complex spatio-temporal relationships, QE-BEV introduces the Top-K Attention mechanism. The mechanism adaptively focuses on clusters of highly relevant features in the context, significantly enhancing the expressive power of BEV features. Additionally, the paper proposes a diversity loss function that pays attention to both important and less prominent features, further improving the model’s robustness in complex environments. BEV-IO enhances the BEV representation through instance occupancy information. Specifically, the method performs self-attention operations based on the occupancy distribution of each ray in the frustum, thereby effectively aggregating the geometric cues of instances into the image features. This significantly improves the expressiveness and semantic richness of the BEV features.

#### 3.4.3. Output feature optimization

The optimization of output features aims to post-process the generated BEV features, making them more suitable for downstream tasks. However, key challenges such as the difficulty in perceiving small targets and the decline in cross-domain generalization performance still exist in real-world scenarios. Factors such as perceptual noise, information sparsity, and object occlusion often affect the accuracy of small object detection, posing potential threats to the safety of autonomous driving. To address these issues, BEV-TinySpotter introduces multi-scale spatial information, significantly enhancing the feature recognition capability for small objects. Additionally, this method incorporates temporal sequence information to capture the distribution changes and movement trends of small objects across consecutive frames, improving the understanding of their dynamic characteristics. On the other hand, different domains may have varying data distributions or environmental conditions. To mitigate the impact of performance degradation across domains, BEV-Seg generates cross-domain intermediate representations by abstracting high-level geometric features, effectively addressing the performance degradation issues caused by domain differences in traditional methods. The related approach can adapt to different domains and maintain good performance in new domains while reducing dependence on large-scale labeled data, thereby significantly enhancing the reliability and accuracy of the output features.

To further improve depth perception, EA-BEV optimizes depth estimation through its edge-aware deep fusion module. This module leverages edge information to enhance the depth reasoning of object contours, improving the local details of depth estimation. Such enhancement optimizes the BEV feature representation and bolsters the reliability of output features in terms of spatial consistency and geometric accuracy. BEVRefiner proposes a deep refinement method that accurately predicts the depth information of each pixel by integrating 3D context information and prior knowledge of depth distribution, enhancing the quality of the generated BEV features. Most existing methods aggregate multi-view camera features into the BEV grid, but these approaches may ignore the feature differences of targets at different heights, leading to information loss or confusion. To alleviate the issue of information loss, BEV-SAN subdivides the BEV space into multiple height slices, generating global and local BEV slices separately to preserve the feature differences of targets at different heights. Additionally, BEV-SAN utilizes an attention mechanism to dynamically assign weights, highlighting key features, thereby enhancing the accuracy and robustness of the feature representation.

## 4\. Future outlook and challenges

**Multi-modal Fusion** With the development of sensor technologies, future BEV perception techniques will increasingly rely on multi-sensor data fusion, particularly the collaborative work of cameras, LiDAR, and Radar. This multi-sensor fusion can compensate for the limitations of individual sensors in complex environments, enhancing the accuracy, robustness, and reliability of perception. Specifically, LiDAR provides high-precision depth information, maintaining good performance even in low light conditions,. Radar has an advantage in complex weather conditions such as rain, fog, and snow due to its strong penetration capabilities through water vapor and particles in the environment. Cameras, on the other hand, offer high-resolution visual information, making them especially suitable for object recognition and detail capture.

**Generative and 4D BEV Modeling** The BEV perception system will no longer be limited to a single object detection task but is expected to expand to a wider range of perception tasks, such as semantic segmentation, path planning, trajectory prediction, and multi-object tracking. These perception tasks further raise the requirements for the modeling capabilities and generalization performance of BEV systems. To address these challenges, BEV research is evolving toward deeper and more diverse directions. Firstly, diffusion models have been introduced into BEV perception due to their powerful denoising capabilities, enabling the generation of higher-quality BEV representations. Secondly, 4D BEV modeling has also gained increasing attention, as it incorporates the temporal dimension into the traditional 3D spatial framework, empowering autonomous driving systems with a better understanding of dynamic scenes.

**LLM-Augmented BEV Perception** Meanwhile, the integration of large language models (LLMs) with BEV perception has emerged as a new research hotspot. Leveraging their powerful cross-modal understanding and reasoning capabilities, LLMs can significantly enhance the scene understanding ability of BEV systems, enabling more intelligent human–vehicle collaboration. For example, BEVInstructor integrates BEV representations into multi-modal large language models (MLLMs) to generate instructions, thereby enhancing semantic understanding of complex traffic scenarios. BEV-CLIP leverages the textual semantic understanding capabilities of LLMs to enable cross-modal retrieval from text to BEV features, effectively improving recognition and retrieval performance in long-tail scenarios. Talk2BEV combines large vision–language models (LVLMs) with BEV maps, enabling a unified system to perform multi-task collaboration. BEVDriver builds an LLM-based end-to-end closed-loop driving model, significantly enhancing the stability of autonomous driving systems in complex environments. In addition, BEV representations based on occupancy prediction are also rapidly evolving. Occupancy prediction provides complete 3D spatial information, enabling accurate understanding of complex environments.

**Challenges in Advanced BEV Perception** Despite the significant progress of BEV perception in areas such as multi-modal fusion and collaboration with large models, several critical challenges remain to be addressed. On the one hand, although diffusion models have shown remarkable performance in enhancing the quality of BEV representations, their high computational cost during inference and the tendency to produce blurry dynamic object generations limit their real-time capability and robustness. On the other hand, the integration of BEV perception with large language models is still in an exploratory phase. A major challenge lies in effectively aligning 3D spatial data with linguistic reasoning, which can lead to semantic inconsistencies and hinder comprehensive scene understanding. Moreover, occupancy prediction faces efficiency bottlenecks in achieving high-resolution mapping, and most existing methods are limited to single-frame prediction, making it difficult to model dynamic and changing environments. Incorporating multi-frame information to construct an occupancy flow is a key direction for enhancing temporal modeling capabilities and supporting motion understanding. At the same time, deploying BEV models on automotive embedded platforms (e.g.,NVIDIA Orin) still faces constraints in computing power, memory, and power consumption, along with strict requirements for low latency and high throughput in the perception pipeline. Achieving an efficient balance between accuracy and real-time performance thus remains a key challenge for practical deployment.

This paper provides a comprehensive overview of the current status and development trends of BEV perception technology, evaluating and comparing the characteristics and advantages of various methods, as well as presenting relevant datasets and evaluation metrics. We analyze various approaches based on cameras, LiDAR, and multi-sensor fusion. We focus on the advantages and disadvantages of each method, as well as their potential applications in autonomous driving. Additionally, this paper discusses BEV data augmentation techniques, including the filtering of prior information, enhancement of intermediate layer features, and optimization of output features. The paper also elaborates on the future prospects of BEV perception technology and the significant challenges it currently faces. Through comparative and in-depth analysis of various methods, this paper highlights the unique advantages and characteristics of BEV perception technology. It aims to provide inspiration for the development and optimization of future autonomous driving systems and to better identify directions for future research.

[^3]: **Semantic Segmentation** Semantic segmentation plays an indispensable role in BEV perception tasks, which is mainly divided into map segmentation and object segmentation. Map segmentation focuses on extracting static elements such as lane markings and roads to generate BEV maps for path planning,,. Object segmentation targets dynamic elements such as vehicles and pedestrians, providing support for behavior prediction and obstacle avoidance. BEV semantic segmentation not only enhances the depth and breadth of scene understanding but also directly impacts core tasks such as path planning, dynamic decision-making, and multi-modal fusion,. Traditional semantic segmentation is achieved using supervised learning algorithms. Firstly, clustering algorithms are employed to identify the neighborhood of each point for feature extraction, and traditional classifiers are used to perform local classification on each pixel. Then, a conditional random field model is introduced to account for contextual information, enabling global label smoothing and optimization. Finally, the process produces accurate semantic segmentation results. With the rapid development of deep learning, neural networks have demonstrated exceptional efficiency in feature extraction and classification tasks, significantly improving the performance and accuracy of semantic segmentation.

[^6]: While MLP has achieved remarkable results in the view transformation from PV to BEV, it faces challenges in effectively handling the global positional relationships among multiple views. In contrast, the attention mechanism in the Transformer architecture enables cross-view learning, which facilitates a better understanding of global positional information. Furthermore, the robust data dependency and scalability of the Transformer allows them to learn more flexible feature representations, enabling the handling of more complex scenes and adaptation to varying task requirements. CVT embeds positional information through the internal and external calibration of cameras and introduces a cross-view attention mechanism to achieve the projection from camera view to BEV. DETR3D employs a Transformer architecture to enhance BEV features for better predictions. Bev-locator projects multi-view image features into the BEV space. Additionally, the method introduces a cross-modal Transformer module, which infers the ego pose by computing self-attention and cross-attention. DiffBEV proposes an end-to-end framework that utilizes a cross-attention module to merge BEV features. However, the Transformer-based approaches adopt unidirectional and posterior interaction mechanisms, which pose challenges for the transformation from perspective view to bird’s eye view. To address this issue, BAEFormer proposes a bi-directional and early interaction transformers framework, which further enhances the algorithm’s performance by downsampling multiview image features. The implicit methods no longer rely on explicit geometric transformations for the projection from PV to BEV, but instead focus more on training neural networks with large amounts of data to achieve efficient view transformation.

[^7]: In 3D object detection tasks, LiDAR point clouds exhibit an uneven distribution in 3D space, resulting in significant disparities in their contributions to object detection across different spatial regions. During feature transformation, the network dynamically focuses on regions with high data contribution, thereby mitigating information loss and reducing computational overhead. SlENet proposes a spatial information enhancement network that demonstrates robustness to density-imbalanced point clouds, achieving precise 3D object detection.
